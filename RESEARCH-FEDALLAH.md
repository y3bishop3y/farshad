# Fedallah in Moby-Dick — Research Reference

*Compiled for FRASHAD: A Novel*

---

## What Melville Actually Wrote

Fedallah has no backstory, no interior life, no voice of his own in *Moby-Dick*. He is seen only from outside — through Ishmael's narration and the crew's fear. Everything below is what the text gives us. The gaps are enormous. That's the whole point of the novel we're building.

---

## Physical Description

From Chapter 48 ("The First Lowering") and Chapter 50 ("Ahab's Boat and Crew. Fedallah"):

- **"Tall and swart"**
- **"One white tooth evilly protruding from his steel-like lips"**
- Wears a **black Chinese jacket**
- Wears a **"glistening white plaited turban"** made from "his living hair, coiled round and round upon his head"
- **No beard** — his face is described as smooth
- Described with deliberately spectral, death-like imagery
- Called one of **"five dusky phantoms"** who emerge from below decks

Melville's language is consciously demonic. He is writing Fedallah as the crew sees him — as something inhuman. The novel FRASHAD would show who is actually inside that description.

## The Appearance Problem — And Why It's Actually the Answer

### What Melville Got Wrong

Melville almost certainly did not know what a Parsee man actually looked like. His knowledge of Zoroastrians came through Transcendentalist writings (Emerson, Thoreau publishing "Zoroastrian" texts in Massachusetts) and possibly encounters in ports — not from direct knowledge of Parsee dress and custom. Fedallah was "the first fictitious Parsi" in American literature. Melville was inventing from fragments.

**What a Parsee man in 1830s–40s Bombay actually wore:**
- A **topi** (skull cap) or **pheta/paghdi** (cloth turban, typically dark chocolate or maroon, worn over a skull cap)
- The Parsee turban was fabric — a specific Gujarati-influenced style. Never hair.
- Indoor dress: muslin shirt, loose white trousers, cotton waistcoat with sleeves, skull cap, slippers
- Outdoor: long loose coat added over indoor garments
- Head always covered — going bareheaded was considered disrespectful even among equals
- Described as light olive complexion, aquiline nose, bright black eyes, typically a light curling moustache
- Formal occasions: a long double-breasted white cotton coat (jama) gathered at the waist with a broad white sash

**The turban-of-hair is theologically impossible for a practicing Zoroastrian.** In Zoroastrian tradition, hair is classified as **nasu** — dead matter, ritually impure. Cut hair and nail clippings must be disposed of with elaborate ritual: carried away from fire, water, and the faithful, buried in a hole with prayers chanted over it. The entire point of Zoroastrian head covering is to CONTAIN the nasu — to keep hair from falling into ritually clean spaces. A Zoroastrian displaying his hair coiled around his head like a crown is doing the opposite of what his religion requires. It would be an act of deliberate transgression.

**Who actually wears turbans of uncut hair?** Sikhs. The **dastar** (Sikh turban) exists to cover **kesh** — uncut hair, one of the Five Ks of Sikhism. This is a core religious requirement. Melville appears to have conflated Parsees, Sikhs, and a general Orientalist idea of "Eastern man" into one figure.

**The Chinese jacket** — Parsees did not wear Chinese clothing. This suggests Melville was also folding in images of Chinese or Southeast Asian sailors he'd encountered in Pacific ports. A composite, not a portrait.

**Muslims and turbans** — You're right that Muslims don't typically wear the kind of turban Melville describes. In the Arab/Indian Ocean world Fedallah would have moved through, Muslim men wore a **taqiyah/kufi** (skull cap) or sometimes an **imama** (cloth turban) — but not a hair-turban, and not in the style Melville describes.

### Why Melville's Description Is Actually Right — For Fedallah

Here is the reframe: **Melville didn't know what a Parsee looked like. But he accidentally described what Frashad became.**

Frashad was born into a world of strict purity. His head was always covered. His hair was kept short or contained. Stray hairs were disposed of with prayer. The topi, the pheta, the skull cap — these were not fashion. They were purity practice. They were Asha made visible on the body.

Then he was expelled. He lost everything — his father, his community, his fire temple, his name. He walked out of Bombay at eighteen with the clothes on his back and the prayers still running through him involuntarily.

After that, over thirty years on the ocean, he became something else:

- **The hair grows.** He stops cutting it. At first because there is no one to observe the ritual disposal. Then because he doesn't care. Then — maybe — because letting it grow becomes its own act of meaning. The Behāfarīd heretics let their hair grow long as a mark of their break from orthodoxy. Frashad may not know this history, but the gesture is the same: the body refusing the rules that the community imposed.

- **He coils it around his head.** Not a Sikh dastar. Not a Parsee pheta. Not a Muslim imama. Something he invented at sea — a practical solution that became a signature. His own hair, his own dead matter, his own nasu, worn as a crown. A man who was told his hair was unclean wearing it as the most visible part of himself. Either an act of defiance or an act of indifference so complete it looks like defiance.

- **The black Chinese jacket.** Picked up in Canton, 1820. Not Parsee, not Muslim, not European. A garment from the edge of the world he washed up in. He wears what is available, what is practical, what does not belong to any tradition that expelled him.

- **No beard.** Parsee men typically wore moustaches. A smooth face could mean many things — personal preference, practicality at sea, or another marker of his break from the community's norms.

- **The white tooth.** Years of hard living. Poor diet at sea. The body as record.

### The Final Form

What Ishmael sees when Fedallah emerges from the hold is not a Parsee. It is not a Sikh, a Muslim, a Chinese sailor, or a European. It is a man who has traveled more of the world than almost anyone alive in the 1840s — Bombay to the Malabar Coast to Zanzibar to Muscat to Mozambique to Canton to the Pacific to Lahaina to Nantucket — and who has been stripped of every identity along the way.

He speaks English (learned from the British in Bombay ports and on whaling ships). He speaks Arabic (learned in Zanzibar and Muscat). He speaks Swahili, Gujarati, and enough Cantonese to survive Canton. He reads Avestan — a dead language even the priests only memorized phonetically. He is more polyglot than almost anyone on the Pequod.

He dresses in pieces of everywhere he has been. The Chinese jacket from Canton. The hair-turban from no tradition at all — or from every tradition, fused and remade. The black trousers that could be from anywhere.

The crew sees a devil. Stubb sees a "yellow devil." The sailors see an "evil angel." Ishmael sees a "muffled mystery."

What they are actually seeing is a man's final form. A man who spent the first eighteen years of his life in a world where every garment, every hair, every gesture had cosmic meaning — where the Kusti cord was tied three times for good thoughts, good words, good deeds, and the topi covered the nasu, and the sudreh held a pocket near the heart for collecting righteousness — and who then spent the next thirty-four years having all of that stripped away, port by port, ocean by ocean, until what was left was this:

A tall dark man with his own hair coiled around his head, wearing a dead man's jacket from a country he passed through once, standing silently in the bow of a whaleboat with a harpoon in his hands and three true prophecies in his mouth and no one in the world who knows his real name.

That is what Melville saw without understanding it. That is what the novel explains.

## Every Appearance in the Novel

Fedallah appears in approximately **15 chapters**, but speaks in very few. He is mostly a silent presence — standing behind Ahab, watching.

### Key Scenes:

**Chapter 47–48 ("The Mat-Maker" / "The First Lowering")**
Fedallah and four other men emerge from the hold during the first whale chase. The crew is shocked — no one knew they were aboard. They are Ahab's private boat crew, smuggled on before sailing. Fedallah stands in the bow of Ahab's boat as harpooner.

**Chapter 50 ("Ahab's Boat and Crew. Fedallah")**
Ishmael's description chapter. Key line: Fedallah "remained a muffled mystery to the last." His crew does not mingle with the rest of the ship. The Pequod's men generate rumors — he is Ahab's "evil angel," a familiar spirit, the devil himself. Ishmael notes that in old Nantucket whaling tradition, captains sometimes brought their own secret boat crews, so this isn't unprecedented — but Fedallah's appearance makes it feel supernatural.

**Chapter 51 ("The Spirit-Spout")**
Fedallah is the first to spot a mysterious silvery spout at night — a phantom whale that the ship pursues southward. His vigilance and night-watching feed crew superstition. He seems to never sleep.

**Chapter 73 ("Stubb and Flask Kill a Right Whale")**
Stubb speculates that Fedallah has some hold over Ahab — that Ahab keeps the right whale's head on one side and the sperm whale's head on the other because Fedallah told him to, as a charm. Stubb: "I take that Fedallah to be the devil in disguise."

**Chapter 117 ("The Whale Watch")**
THE PROPHECY SCENE. Ahab and Fedallah sit together at night in Ahab's whale boat, watching over a killed whale lashed to the ship. This is where Fedallah delivers the three prophecies:

1. Ahab will see **two hearses** before he dies — the first "not made by mortal hands," the second made of "wood grown in America"
2. Fedallah will **die first** — "I shall still go before thee thy pilot"
3. Only **hemp** (rope) can kill Ahab

Ahab takes this to mean he is essentially immortal. He is wrong.

**Chapter 119 ("The Candles")**
St. Elmo's fire (electrical discharge) blazes on the ship's masts during a typhoon. Ahab addresses the fire directly: "Oh! thou clear spirit of clear fire, whom on these seas I as Persian once did worship..." — suggesting Ahab has some past connection to Zoroastrian fire worship, possibly through Fedallah's influence. Fedallah is present but silent.

**Chapter 130 ("The Hat")**
A sky hawk snatches Ahab's hat from his head — an omen. Fedallah watches. He is described as closely shadowing Ahab at all times now, as if the end is approaching and he knows it.

**Chapter 131 ("The Pequod Meets the Delight")**
The Pequod encounters another ship that has lost five men to Moby Dick. The Delight's captain says no harpoon forged can kill the white whale. Ahab holds up his specially forged harpoon. Fedallah is present, silent.

**Chapters 133–134 ("The Chase — First Day" / "The Chase — Second Day")**
The three-day chase begins. On the second day, Ahab's boat is destroyed by Moby Dick. Fedallah disappears. He is **not found** when the crew is recovered.

**Chapter 135 ("The Chase — Third Day")**
Moby Dick surfaces with **Fedallah's body lashed to its side** — tangled in harpoon lines, wrapped around the whale, dead. His corpse is the "first hearse" — "not made by mortal hands." Ahab sees him:

> "Aye, Parsee! I see thee again. — Aye, and thou goest before; and this, THIS then is the hearse that thou didst promise."

Fedallah has "gone before" as pilot — his dead body on the whale, leading Ahab to his death. All three prophecies fulfilled.

## How the Crew Sees Him

The Pequod's crew treats Fedallah with consistent fear and suspicion:

- **"The devil in disguise"** — Stubb's assessment
- **"Yellow devils" / "yellow boys"** — racial slurs from the crew
- Rumors that Ahab has **"sold his soul"** and Fedallah is the price
- He is compared to a **familiar spirit** — a demon servant in European witchcraft tradition
- He never mingles with other crew. He doesn't eat with them, sleep where they sleep, or speak to them
- Unlike the other non-white harpooners (Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo) who are individualized and given personality, Fedallah is kept deliberately opaque

**The Orientalism problem:** Scholars note that Melville treats his other "exotic" characters with relative dignity — Queequeg gets a full interior life, humor, warmth. Fedallah gets none. He is "degraded racial characteristics" and denied an individual voice (scholar reading). Whether Melville intended this as a commentary on how the West perceives the East, or whether he simply couldn't imagine past the stereotype, is debated. Either way — the silence is the space the novel fills.

## The Ahab-Fedallah Bond

### What the Text Shows:
- Fedallah is loyal only to Ahab. He obeys no one else.
- He was brought aboard secretly — suggesting a prior relationship, arranged before the voyage
- He stands behind Ahab on the quarterdeck at night — described as shadow and substance
- He watches the sea when Ahab watches the sea. He sleeps (if he sleeps) when Ahab sleeps.
- He delivers prophecy to Ahab — and only to Ahab
- He does not warn Ahab. He does not try to change the outcome. He simply tells him what will happen.

### What the Text Does NOT Show:
- How they met
- When they met
- Why Fedallah agreed to come
- What Fedallah thinks about any of this
- Whether Fedallah wants to survive
- Whether Fedallah is afraid
- Whether Fedallah believes his own prophecies or is performing them
- What Fedallah's life was before Ahab

## Fedallah's Death

On the second day of the three-day chase, Moby Dick destroys Ahab's boat. Fedallah goes into the water. He does not resurface. On the third day, the whale comes up with Fedallah's corpse tangled in the harpoon lines, lashed to the whale's white body.

Melville's language: Fedallah's body is **pinioned** — bound, restrained, fixed to the whale. He has become part of the thing they were hunting. His corpse rides the whale like a figurehead rides a ship.

The crew sees a dead man on the whale before Ahab sees him. When Ahab finally looks:

> "Befooled, befooled! — Ha! the Parsee — lashed to the whale!"

This is the moment Ahab understands the prophecies. The first hearse is the whale, carrying Fedallah's body. The second hearse will be the Pequod. Hemp will be the line around his own neck. The end has arrived and it looks exactly as Fedallah said it would.

## The Sorcery / Prophet Question

This matters for deciding WHY Frashad was expelled from his community.

### The Zoroastrian Framework for Sorcery:

- A **yatu** is a sorcerer in Zoroastrian theology — a practitioner of **Yatukih** (sorcery), considered a servant of Angra Mainyu (the evil spirit)
- Yatus are described as shape-shifters, nomadic, practitioners of **daeva-yasna** (demon worship)
- The **Vendidad** ("Law Against Demons") explicitly condemns sorcery and unauthorized supernatural practice
- The attitude toward sorcery in Zoroastrian tradition is **"wholly hostile"**
- Sorcery is not merely a sin — it is ontologically aligned with Druj (chaos, the Lie). A sorcerer is not someone who does bad things; a sorcerer IS Druj in human form.

### Historical Precedent — Behāfarīd (8th century):

A Zoroastrian who claimed prophetic authority outside the priesthood. He:
- Started a religious reform movement blending Zoroastrian and Islamic elements
- Had followers who modified prayer practices
- Was **executed by hanging** (by Abbasid authorities, but with Zoroastrian priestly approval)
- His followers believed he would return — showing that unsanctioned prophecy created lasting rupture

### What This Could Mean for Frashad:

If Frashad — the son of a Mobed, a boy trained to tend sacred fire — began showing prophetic abilities outside the sanctioned priestly framework, the community response would be severe. Not because prophecy itself is forbidden (the Saoshyant is a prophesied messiah), but because:

1. **Unauthorized prophecy = sorcery.** If it doesn't come through proper channels (the priesthood, the Avestan tradition), it comes from Druj. Period.
2. **A priest's son who sees visions is especially dangerous.** He has access to sacred fire, sacred rituals, sacred knowledge. If that access is being "used" by dark forces, the contamination could spread.
3. **The Panchayat wouldn't deliberate.** A boy having visions, making predictions that come true, speaking things he shouldn't know — in 1801 Bombay, this is not a theological debate. This is a threat to the community's spiritual purity.
4. **His father's position makes it worse, not better.** A Mobed's son is supposed to be the purest of the pure. If the Mobed's own child is channeling Druj, what does that say about the Mobed? About his fire? About the temple?

### The Alternative Exile Story:

Instead of (or in addition to) sexual transgression, Frashad's exile could be driven by prophetic vision:

- He begins seeing things. Maybe dreams. Maybe waking visions. Predictions that come true.
- At first this might seem like a gift — a sign of spiritual depth. His father might even be proud.
- Then the visions get darker. He sees things he shouldn't. He predicts deaths. He knows things about people — private things, shameful things.
- The community shifts from wonder to fear. This doesn't feel like Asha. This feels like something else.
- The Panchayat consults. The word **yatu** is spoken.
- His father cannot defend him. To defend a yatu would make the Mobed complicit.
- Frashad runs — or is driven out — not because he did something wrong, but because he IS something wrong, in their framework. He is a boy the universe is speaking through, and his community can only hear the devil.

### Why This Might Work Better Than the Sexual Exile:

- It connects directly to Fedallah's role in *Moby-Dick* — he IS a prophet on the Pequod. The prophecies are real. They come true. If the novel shows that this ability was always there, it recontextualizes everything.
- It makes the crew's fear of him on the Pequod an echo of his community's fear in Bombay. He has been called a devil before. Being called one again on a Nantucket whaling ship is just the pattern repeating.
- It deepens the Asha/Druj wound. His prophetic gift might actually BE Asha — cosmic truth flowing through him. But it looks like Druj to everyone around him. The question of whether his visions are divine or demonic is never answered. Not in Bombay, not on the Pequod, not in the novel.
- It gives him a reason to seek out Ahab. Frashad has spent his whole life seeing things. He has learned to keep silent. Then he hears about the Essex — a whale that struck a ship on purpose — and he recognizes something. He has been seeing a white shape in dark water. He knows what's coming. He has always known.

### These Two Reasons Don't Have to Be Mutually Exclusive:

The sexual element and the prophetic element could coexist. Maybe the visions start around the same time as his feelings for the boy. Maybe the community conflates them — sees both as evidence of corruption. Maybe Frashad himself can't separate them — the most true thing he's ever felt and the most frightening thing he's ever seen arrive together, and he can't tell which one damned him.

This is something to keep thinking about. Nothing is locked in.

## Open Questions

1. **Does Fedallah choose to die?** He walks into the three-day chase knowing his own prophecy says he dies first. Is this sacrifice? Acceptance? Fulfillment of duty?

2. **What does Fedallah see in Ahab?** The text shows Ahab's obsession clearly. But what is Fedallah's obsession? Does he have one? Or has he reached a state past obsession — past wanting — and Ahab is simply the last thing the universe put in his path?

3. **Does Fedallah believe in his own prophecies?** Does he experience them as divine revelation? As Asha speaking through him? Or as a curse — the thing that got him expelled, the thing he can never stop?

4. **What happens in the water?** On the second day of the chase, Fedallah goes into the ocean and doesn't come back up. Does he struggle? Does he let go? Is there a moment, tangled in lines, pulled down by the whale, where the Avestan prayers come one last time?

5. **What is his last word?** The novel outline says: **Asha.** Is that right? Does he die returning to truth? Or does he die still unsure?
