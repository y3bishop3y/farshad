# The Four Harpooneers — Research Reference

*Compiled for FRASHAD: A Novel*

*Why four? What do they represent? What is Fedallah's place among them?*

---

## The Four

| Harpooneer | Origin | Continent | Serves Under | How He Arrives |
|------------|--------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Queequeg** | Rokovoko (fictional Polynesian island) | Pacific / Oceania | Starbuck (1st mate) | Boards at New Bedford with Ishmael — the first character we meet |
| **Tashtego** | Gay Head, Martha's Vineyard (Wampanoag) | Americas | Stubb (2nd mate) | Part of the regular crew — American indigenous |
| **Daggoo** | Coastal village (Africa) | Africa | Flask (3rd mate) | Part of the regular crew — voluntarily joined a whaling ship as a youth |
| **Fedallah** | India (Parsee / Zoroastrian) | Asia | Ahab (Captain) | Smuggled aboard secretly — emerges from the hold with four other men during the first whale chase |

Four harpooneers. Four continents. Four non-white men serving under white New England officers. Not one of them American-born in the European sense. And you're right — there are no accidents here.

---

## The Structure: Knights and Squires

Melville names his framework explicitly in Chapters 26–27 ("Knights and Squires"). Each mate is a "knight" — a white New England officer. Each harpooneer is his "squire" — a non-white man who stands beside him in the boat and does the killing.

| Knight (White, American) | Squire (Non-white, Foreign) |
|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Starbuck** — Quaker, Nantucket, cautious, moral | **Queequeg** — Polynesian prince, cannibal, warm, loyal |
| **Stubb** — Cape Cod, easygoing, jovial, crude | **Tashtego** — Wampanoag, skilled, proud, indigenous |
| **Flask** — Martha's Vineyard, small, aggressive, literal-minded | **Daggoo** — African, enormous, powerful, dignified |
| **Ahab** — Nantucket, monomaniacal, doomed | **Fedallah** — Parsee, prophetic, silent, spectral |

The white men command. The non-white men kill. Melville is explicit about this being a metaphor for racial hierarchy in America. But he's also doing something else — each pairing is a kind of doubling, where the harpooneer reveals something about the mate he serves.

---

## How They Arrive — And Why It Matters

You noticed something important: they don't all arrive the same way.

**Queequeg** — Arrives BEFORE the ship. He's the first major character Ishmael meets, at the Spouter-Inn in New Bedford. They share a bed. They become "married" (Melville's word). Queequeg is the most fully human character in the novel — he has warmth, humor, a backstory (a prince who left his island to learn about Christians), and a physical presence that is described with love, not fear. He boards the Pequod openly, as a regular crew member. He belongs.

**Tashtego and Daggoo** — Part of the regular crew. They're introduced in Chapter 27 alongside their mates. They're present, visible, professional. They have distinct personalities — Tashtego is proud and skilled, Daggoo is enormous and dignified — but they're integrated into the ship's normal hierarchy.

**Fedallah** — Smuggled aboard. Hidden below decks. Emerges from the hold during the first whale chase (Chapters 47–48) like something being born from the ship's belly. He and his four men are Ahab's SECRET crew — loyal only to the captain, outside the normal command structure. The regular crew is shocked and frightened. No one knew they were there.

The pattern: the first three harpooneers are part of the ship's known world. Fedallah is from below — from the hidden, the secret, the unconscious. He doesn't arrive at the ship. He was always inside it, waiting.

## What They Represent

### The Four Continents Reading

The most straightforward reading: Melville is putting the entire non-European world on one ship.

- **Queequeg** = The Pacific / Oceania
- **Tashtego** = The Americas (indigenous)
- **Daggoo** = Africa
- **Fedallah** = Asia

The Pequod becomes what Melville calls "the common continent of men... federated along one keel." Every race, every continent, yoked together on a single ship commanded by white Americans headed toward destruction. This is Melville's vision of America itself — a multiracial project driven by a monomaniacal white captain toward an apocalypse no one except the non-white prophet can see coming.

### The Four Elements Reading

Scholars haven't fully developed this, but the resonances are there:

| Harpooneer | Element | Evidence |
|------------|---------|----------|
| **Queequeg** | **Water** | His coffin becomes the life-buoy. He is associated with the sea throughout. His body is covered in tattoos that chart the cosmos — "a complete theory of the heavens and the earth, and a mystical treatise on the art of attaining truth." Water is the element of survival, of truth, of passage. |
| **Tashtego** | **Earth** | Wampanoag — indigenous to the American earth. Son of a people the Europeans displaced. Falls into a whale's head and is pulled out (Chapter 78) — literally swallowed by the animal world and reborn. At the end, he's on the mast, nailing the flag to the spar as the ship sinks — the last man standing on the highest point of the ship, between earth and sky. |
| **Daggoo** | **Air** | The tallest, most physically imposing figure on the ship. Flask literally stands on his shoulders to see further — Daggoo lifts him into the air. In Chapter 40 ("Midnight, Forecastle"), Daggoo rises to his full height in a confrontation and dominates the space. He is associated with height, elevation, physical presence above others. |
| **Fedallah** | **Fire** | Zoroastrian fire-worshipper. Associated with the tryworks flame. Ahab's "as Persian once did worship" speech in the candles scene (Chapter 119). Fire is the element of prophecy, of sacred truth, and of destruction. The whale oil burns in the tryworks. The ship burns symbolically. Fedallah is the fire that consumes. |

### The Four Horsemen Reading

You asked about this directly. The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse (Revelation 6:1–8):

| Horseman | Color | Represents | Possible Harpooneer |
|----------|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **First** | White | Conquest (or False Christ) | **Fedallah** — wears a "glistening white" turban, serves the conquering Ahab, brings false hope (the prophecies sound like immortality but mean death), associated with the white whale itself |
| **Second** | Red | War / Bloodshed | **Tashtego** — the warrior, the hunter, the one who nails the flag to the mast in a final act of defiance, descendant of a people destroyed by conquest, the color of war and blood |
| **Third** | Black | Famine / Scarcity | **Daggoo** — "a gigantic, coal-black" man, the most physically powerful but the most exploited, carries the scales of racial injustice (Flask stands on his shoulders — literally weighing him down), associated with economic extraction (whale oil = the commodity that drives everything) |
| **Fourth** | Pale | Death | **Queequeg** — the one who builds his own coffin, who sickens nearly to death and recovers, whose coffin becomes the life-buoy, whose body is a "mystical treatise" written in death-symbols. He is the horseman called Death — but inverted. His death-object saves the only survivor. |

This isn't a neat one-to-one allegory — Melville doesn't work that way. But the resonances are real:
- Four figures of destruction
- Each associated with a different aspect of apocalypse
- Each riding alongside a "seal" being opened (Ahab's monomaniacal quest as the opening of seals)
- The last one is Death, followed by Hades — and the Pequod's sinking IS Hades, swallowing everything

### Why Four

Beyond continents and horsemen, the number four has structural weight across traditions:

- **Four cardinal directions** — the harpooneers come from every direction (south Pacific, east Asia, west Africa, the Americas)
- **Four seasons** — if mapped: Queequeg = spring (renewal, the coffin-as-life), Tashtego = summer (the warrior in full power), Daggoo = autumn (the harvest, the extraction), Fedallah = winter (death, prophecy, the end)
- **Four Amesha Spentas** (Zoroastrian) — though there are seven, four of them guard the physical elements (fire, water, earth, plants). Fedallah's tradition would recognize this pattern.
- **Four Gospels** — Matthew, Mark, Luke, John. Four witnesses to the same truth, each telling it differently. Each harpooneer witnesses the same doom from a different position.

Melville knew his Bible, his mythology, and his classical education. The number four is not random.

## The Death Order

This matters for FRASHAD. Who dies first, and what does the sequence mean?

| Order | Who | How | When |
|-------|-----|-----|------|
| **1st** | **Fedallah** | Caught in whale lines, dragged under by Moby Dick. Body surfaces lashed to the whale. | Day 2 of the chase |
| **2nd** | **Ahab** | Hemp line catches around his neck, shoots him out of the boat. Dragged under. | Day 3 of the chase |
| **3rd** | **Tashtego** | On the mainmast nailing Ahab's flag as the ship sinks. Catches a sky-hawk between hammer and wood. Goes down with the ship, dragging "a living part of heaven" with him. | Day 3, as the Pequod sinks |
| **4th** | **Queequeg, Daggoo, Starbuck, Stubb, Flask, and the rest** | Go down with the ship. The vortex swallows everything. | Day 3, the sinking |
| **Survivor** | **Ishmael** | Thrown from Ahab's boat during the chase. Floats at the margin. Queequeg's coffin-turned-life-buoy shoots up from the depths. He clings to it for a day and night. Rescued by the Rachel. | After the sinking |

**Fedallah dies first.** This fulfills his own prophecy ("I shall still go before thee thy pilot"). But it also means something structurally: the prophet goes first. The one who SEES the end is the first to enter it. He doesn't watch the apocalypse — he opens it.

**Tashtego dies last** (of those we see die individually). His is the most visually stunning death — nailing the flag, catching the hawk, going under with the ship dragging heaven down with it. The indigenous American is the last man standing on the sinking ship of American empire.

**Queequeg is already dead** before the final chase — he died of his illness earlier. But his COFFIN survives everything. The Pacific Islander's death-box becomes the only thing that floats. Life from death. The cannibal's coffin saves the American narrator.

**Daggoo** — his specific death is not individuated. He goes down with the ship, unnamed in the final passage. The African disappears into the collective. Melville doesn't give him a dramatic death scene. This is either a failure of imagination or a devastating commentary: the Black man's death is not considered worth narrating individually.

## Fedallah Among Them

Here's what makes Fedallah different from the other three:

### He Is Not Part of the Ship's Community

Queequeg, Tashtego, and Daggoo are integrated into the Pequod's social world. They eat with the crew (at a separate table, served after the officers — Melville describes this in detail). They participate in the forecastle scenes. They interact with their mates. Queequeg has Ishmael. Tashtego has his Wampanoag pride. Daggoo has his physical dominance.

Fedallah has nothing. He doesn't eat with the crew. He doesn't sleep where they sleep. He doesn't speak to them. He is Ahab's, and Ahab's alone. He exists in a separate orbit — closer to the captain than anyone, further from the crew than anyone.

### He Is the Only One Who Knows

The other three harpooneers go into the chase without foreknowledge. They are brave, skilled, professional. They don't know they're going to die.

Fedallah knows. He has prophesied his own death. He has told Ahab what will happen. He walks into the three-day chase with full knowledge that he will die first, that his body will surface lashed to the whale, that Ahab will follow him. He is the only person on the ship — including Ahab — who understands the ending completely.

### He Is the Only One Associated with a Specific Religious Tradition

Queequeg's religion is invented — a kind of generalized "pagan" worship of a small idol named Yojo. It is treated with humor and affection. Tashtego's spiritual world is implied but not explored. Daggoo's inner life is barely sketched.

Fedallah carries an entire theological system — Zoroastrianism, with its cosmic dualism, its sacred fire, its prophecy tradition. He is the only harpooneer whose religion Melville specifically names and engages with. The Asha/Druj framework, the fire worship, the prophetic structure — these are ACTIVE in the novel's theology in a way the other harpooneers' traditions are not.

### He Is the Only One the Crew Fears

Queequeg frightens Ishmael initially but quickly becomes beloved. Tashtego is respected. Daggoo intimidates physically but is not feared supernaturally.

Fedallah is called the devil. Repeatedly. By multiple crew members. He is the only one the crew believes has supernatural power — the only one they think is genuinely dangerous beyond his physical abilities. The other three are exotic. Fedallah is uncanny.

## The Relationships Between Them

### What the Text Shows

Very little. The harpooneers don't interact much with each other as a group. The key moments:

**Chapter 36 ("The Quarter-Deck"):** Ahab addresses all three regular harpooneers — Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo — when revealing Moby Dick and rallying them to the hunt. They respond enthusiastically. Fedallah is not present (still hidden below decks).

**Chapter 40 ("Midnight, Forecastle"):** A multi-voice scene on the forecastle. Daggoo gets into a confrontation with a Spanish sailor who calls his blackness "the dark side of humanity." Tashtego is present but less prominent. Queequeg is mentioned. Fedallah is absent — he does not participate in the crew's social world.

**Chapters 133–135 (The Chase):** All four harpooneers are active in the final chase. Queequeg, Tashtego, and Daggoo go up the masts as lookouts when their boats are destroyed. Fedallah is in Ahab's boat. They are in the same physical space but operating in different narrative registers — the three are part of the ship's collective effort, Fedallah is part of Ahab's private doom.

### What the Text Does NOT Show

- Any conversation between Fedallah and the other harpooneers
- Whether they regard him with the same fear the white crew does
- Whether Queequeg — himself from a "pagan" tradition, himself initially feared by Ishmael — recognizes something in Fedallah
- Whether Daggoo or Tashtego have any opinion of the Parsee at all
- Any scene where the four harpooneers are together as equals, outside the command structure

This absence is itself significant. The three regular harpooneers have a kind of fraternity — they sit together at meals, they share the forecastle, they are part of the ship's community even if marginalized within it. Fedallah is not part of this fraternity. He exists in the space between Ahab and everyone else.

## Questions for the Novel

1. **Does Frashad/Fedallah see the other harpooneers as kin?** Three non-white men doing the killing while white men give the orders — does he recognize this structure? Does he see himself as part of it, or apart from it?

2. **Does Queequeg see Fedallah?** Queequeg is the most spiritually open character on the ship — he has his own religion, his own cosmology, his own relationship to death (he builds his own coffin). Does he perceive something in Fedallah that the white crew cannot? Two "pagans" on a Christian ship — do they recognize each other?

3. **What does Fedallah think of the harpooneers' role?** He has been killing whales for decades. He has watched brown and Black men do the killing while white men profit. Does this register for him? Or has he moved past caring about human hierarchies?

4. **The four continents problem:** Fedallah has BEEN to multiple continents. He is not simply "Asia." He is Bombay, Zanzibar, Muscat, Mozambique, Canton, the Pacific, Lahaina, Nantucket. He contains more of the world than any other character on the ship. When he stands in the bow of Ahab's boat as one of four harpooneers from four corners of the earth — does he know he's been to all their corners?

5. **The death order:** Fedallah dies first. He goes before, as pilot. The prophet opens the apocalypse. But from Frashad's perspective — from inside, not from Ishmael's narration — what is the last day like? Does he look at the other three on the masts? Does he know they're going to follow him?

## Job 1:19 — The Four Corners and the Four Harpooneers

### The Epigraph

Melville chose Job 1:19 as the epigraph for *Moby-Dick*:

> **"And I only am escaped alone to tell thee."**

This is spoken by a servant — the sole survivor of a catastrophe — who runs to tell Job what happened. What happened is this:

> "And, behold, there came a great wind from the wilderness, and smote the **four corners** of the house, and it fell upon the young men, and they are dead; and I only am escaped alone to tell thee."

A wind from the wilderness strikes the **four corners** of the house. The house collapses. Everyone inside dies. One person escapes to tell the story.

### The Parallel

| Job 1:19 | Moby-Dick |
|----------|-----------|
| A great wind from the wilderness | Moby Dick — the white force from the deep |
| Smote the **four corners** of the house | The Pequod carries **four harpooneers from four corners of the earth** |
| The house fell upon the young men | The ship sinks — everyone goes down |
| "They are dead" | All die except Ishmael |
| "I only am escaped alone to tell thee" | Ishmael survives, floating on Queequeg's coffin, to write the novel |

The Pequod **is** Job's house. The four harpooneers **are** the four corners. Queequeg (Pacific), Tashtego (Americas), Daggoo (Africa), Fedallah (Asia) — four men from the four edges of the world, holding the structure together. When the whale strikes, the house collapses. The four corners fall. One servant escapes.

Melville told the reader this on the title page. Before Chapter 1. Before Ishmael says "Call me." The first thing the reader sees is a survivor of a four-cornered collapse saying: I alone survived to tell you.

### Job vs. Ahab — The Test

Job is tested by God. Satan says to God: Job only worships you because you gave him everything. Take it away and he'll curse you. God agrees. Job loses his livestock, his servants, his children, his health. He is covered in boils, sitting in ashes, scraping his skin with a broken pot.

**Job's wife says: "Curse God and die."**

**Job refuses.** "Shall we receive good from God, and shall we not receive evil?" He does not curse God. He questions. He laments. He demands an answer. But he does not curse.

**Ahab curses.** That is the entire difference. Ahab receives the same test — the whale takes his leg the way the wind takes Job's children — and instead of refusing to curse God, Ahab builds his entire existence around cursing God. "I'd strike the sun if it insulted me." He baptizes his harpoon "in nomine diaboli" — in the name of the devil. He stands on Fedallah and shouts at the fire. He is Job's wife's advice made flesh: *curse God and die.*

Job endures. Ahab attacks. Job is restored (God gives him new children, new wealth). Ahab is destroyed.

And the sun — **the sun that Ahab would strike** — is the sacred object in Zoroastrianism. Frashad's religion. The fire Frashad's father tended is the earthly representation of the sun's divine light. When Ahab says "I'd strike the sun if it insulted me," Frashad hears a man threatening to strike the holiest thing in his cosmos. Ahab is not just cursing God. He is cursing Frashad's God, specifically, by name, without knowing it.

### Frashad as the Opposite of Job's Response

Job was tested and did not curse. He accepted suffering as part of the order — "the Lord gave, and the Lord hath taken away; blessed be the name of the Lord."

Frashad accepts too. Not with Job's words — he has no audience to perform faith for — but with the same posture. Ein breira. What choice do we have? The order is the order. Asha is Asha. You do not strike the sun. You do not curse the fire. You tend it, even when it burns you.

Ahab is Job-who-cursed. Frashad is Job-who-didn't. Two responses to the same cosmic test, standing on the same deck, heading for the same whale.

## The Ship of the Dead — The Pequod and the Dying Religion

### What "Pequod" Means

The ship is named after the **Pequot** — an Algonquian-speaking Native American tribe from Connecticut. Melville knew exactly what he was doing.

**The Pequot War (1636–1638):** English colonists, allied with the Mohegan and Narragansett, attacked the Pequot people. On May 26, 1637, Captain John Mason's forces attacked the fortified Pequot village at Mystic. They set the wooden palisade on fire, blocked the exits, and shot anyone who tried to escape. Between **400 and 700 Pequot were killed** — men, women, children. Survivors were enslaved, sold to Bermuda and the West Indies, or dispersed among other tribes.

**The Treaty of Hartford (1638):** After the massacre, the English didn't just defeat the Pequot. They tried to **erase them.** The treaty prohibited the surviving Pequot from:
- Returning to their lands
- Speaking their tribal language
- **Calling themselves Pequot**

The name itself was outlawed. The colonists tried to kill the people and then kill the word. (The Pequot survived despite this — two branches exist today — but Melville, writing in 1851, called them "now extinct as the ancient Medes.")

**Melville named his doomed ship after a people who were massacred and then erased.** The Pequod carries its own doom in its name. Every time a character says the ship's name, they are invoking a genocide.

### Fedallah on a Ship Named for a Dead People

Now put Fedallah on that ship.

He is a Zoroastrian. One of the last of a dying religion. The numbers:

- After the Arab conquest of Iran (642 CE), Zoroastrians were converted, persecuted, massacred, and driven into exile
- By the 1850s (the era of *Moby-Dick*), fewer than **7,000 Zoroastrians remained in Iran**
- The Parsi community in India peaked at **114,000 in 1941** and has declined to roughly **57,000 by 2011**
- Today, the global Zoroastrian population is estimated at **100,000–120,000** — one of the smallest religious communities on Earth
- The Indian government launched the "Jiyo Parsi" ("Live Parsi") scheme in 2013 to try to prevent the community's extinction

Zoroastrianism is not extinct. But it has been dying for over a thousand years. It was one of the great religions of the ancient world — the state religion of three successive Persian empires, a faith that influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. And it was broken by conquest, reduced to a handful of refugees in western India, and has been shrinking ever since.

**Frashad is a member of a dying faith, standing on a ship named for a dead people, sailing toward the destruction of everyone on board.**

The Pequot were massacred and then their name was outlawed. The Zoroastrians were conquered and then slowly disappeared. Both peoples were real. Both were erased — or nearly. And Melville put a representative of one on a ship named after the other.

### The Layers

| The Pequot | The Zoroastrians | The Pequod |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Massacred by colonists (1637) | Conquered by Arabs (642 CE) | Destroyed by the whale |
| Name outlawed by treaty | Religion slowly dying | Ship's name = memorial to the dead |
| Survivors dispersed, forced to take other names | Survivors fled to India, took new cultural forms | Crew scattered — only Ishmael survives |
| English tried to erase them | Islam absorbed/marginalized them | The ocean erases the ship entirely |
| Two branches survive today | ~100,000 remain worldwide | One man (Ishmael) survives to tell the story |

**Ishmael is the Pequod's Ishmael — the one who survives to tell the story, the way the Pequot's descendants survived despite the erasure.** But Fedallah is different. He doesn't survive. He goes first. He is the first to die on a ship named for a people who were nearly destroyed, and he belongs to a religion that is slowly following the same path.

### What Frashad Would Know

Would Frashad know what "Pequod" means? Almost certainly not. He wouldn't know about the Pequot, about Connecticut, about the massacre at Mystic. The name would be just a name to him — an English word, like so many others he's absorbed.

But the reader would know. And the novel would know. The resonance is there whether Frashad is aware of it or not: a man from a dying faith on a ship named for a dead tribe, sailing into an ending that was determined before anyone boarded.

And Tashtego — the Wampanoag harpooneer from Martha's Vineyard — **he** might know. The Wampanoag had their own relationship to English colonial violence. The Wampanoag fought King Philip's War (1675–1676), one generation after the Pequot War. They know what it means to be a people the colonists tried to destroy. If Tashtego ever thinks about the name of the ship he's on — and Melville never tells us whether he does — he's standing on a grave.

Two indigenous men and a Zoroastrian, all on a ship named for a genocide, all serving under white New England officers. The ship is a coffin before it leaves port. It was always a coffin. Its name says so.
