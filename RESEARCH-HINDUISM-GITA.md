# Hinduism, the Bhagavad Gita, and the Krishna-Arjuna Parallel

*Compiled for FRASHAD: A Novel*

*Krishna drives Arjuna to war. Fedallah drives Ahab to the whale. The charioteer and the harpooner.*

---

## The Parallel

You saw something scholars haven't fully explored: the structural mirror between the Bhagavad Gita and Moby-Dick.

| Bhagavad Gita | Moby-Dick |
|---------------|-----------|
| **Krishna** — divine charioteer, avatar of Vishnu, guides Arjuna to battle | **Fedallah** — harpooner, prophet, shadow, drives Ahab toward the whale |
| **Arjuna** — warrior prince, hesitates before the fight, must be persuaded | **Ahab** — captain, never hesitates, must be warned (and ignores the warning) |
| The chariot | The whaleboat |
| Kurukshetra (the battlefield) | The Pacific Ocean |
| Krishna reveals his true form — Arjuna sees all men already dead | Fedallah's prophecy — he has already seen the ending |
| Krishna says: "I am Time, the destroyer of worlds. These warriors are already slain." | Fedallah says: "I shall still go before thee thy pilot." He already knows who dies. |

The roles are inverted but the structure holds: a divine (or prophetic) figure rides beside a warrior and shows him truth. In the Gita, the warrior is changed by the revelation. In Moby-Dick, the warrior refuses to be changed. That refusal is what destroys the ship.

## The Bhagavad Gita — Chapter 11: The Vishvarupa

### The Setup

The Bhagavad Gita takes place on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, moments before a civil war. Prince Arjuna is in his chariot. His charioteer is Krishna — his friend, his cousin, his guide. Arjuna doesn't know yet that Krishna is an avatar of Vishnu, the supreme god.

Arjuna looks across the battlefield and sees his own family on both sides — uncles, teachers, cousins. He breaks down. He can't fight. He drops his bow and says: I would rather die than kill these men.

Krishna's response IS the Bhagavad Gita — 700 verses of theology, philosophy, and cosmic truth delivered in a chariot on the edge of battle.

### Krishna Is the Eighth Avatar

You remembered correctly. Krishna is the **eighth avatar** (incarnation) of **Vishnu**, the Preserver — the god who sustains the universe. The ten avatars of Vishnu descend to earth whenever cosmic order (dharma) is threatened. Krishna appears when the world needs to be set right.

The avatars:
1. Matsya (fish)
2. Kurma (tortoise)
3. Varaha (boar)
4. Narasimha (man-lion)
5. Vamana (dwarf)
6. Parashurama (warrior with an axe)
7. Rama (prince of Ayodhya)
8. **Krishna** (the charioteer, the teacher)
9. Buddha (in some lists)
10. Kalki (the future avatar, not yet arrived — the one who will end the age)

### The Revelation — Chapter 11

Arjuna asks Krishna to show his true form. Krishna grants him divine vision. What Arjuna sees:

> The form has **unlimited mouths, unlimited eyes, unlimited arms**. It extends immeasurably in all directions. It has no beginning and no end.

> Within this single form, Arjuna sees **all of creation** — every being, every god, every planet, every blade of grass. Past, present, and future exist simultaneously.

> The radiance is compared to **"a thousand suns blazing together."** (This is the verse Oppenheimer quoted at the Trinity nuclear test.)

> The warriors of both armies — Arjuna's enemies AND his allies — are rushing into the mouths of this form **like moths rushing toward fire to perish.** They are already being consumed.

Then the verse that matters most — **Chapter 11, Verse 32:**

> **"I am Time (kala), the destroyer of worlds. With or without your involvement, I will destroy the warriors in both these armies."**

And:

> **"I have already slain your foes — you are but a mere instrument in their destruction."**

### What Arjuna Sees

**All men are already dead.** The battle hasn't started yet, but in Krishna's cosmic form, it's already over. The warriors are already consumed. The outcome is already determined. Arjuna's choice — to fight or not to fight — doesn't change the result. Time has already done its work. He is being asked not to cause death but to participate in a death that has already happened.

Arjuna's hair stands on end. His heart becomes unstable with fear. He begs Krishna to return to his gentle human form. Krishna does.

## The Mirror: Fedallah and Krishna

### The Charioteer and the Harpooner

Krishna drives Arjuna's chariot. Fedallah stands in the bow of Ahab's whaleboat.

Both occupy the position of the guide — the one who brings the warrior to the confrontation. Neither is the fighter. Krishna doesn't pick up a weapon. Fedallah doesn't command the chase. They drive. They steer. They deliver the man to his fate.

### The Prophecy and the Vishvarupa

When Fedallah delivers the three prophecies in Chapter 117, he is doing a compressed version of what Krishna does in Chapter 11 of the Gita:

**He is showing Ahab that the ending is already determined.**

- The two hearses are already built (one is the whale, one is the ship)
- Fedallah has already died (he says "I shall still go before thee")
- The hemp is already around Ahab's neck

Like Krishna, Fedallah is saying: *this has already happened. You are not choosing your death. You are walking into a death that was arranged before you got here.*

Ahab, like Arjuna, is being told: **you are an instrument, not a cause.**

But here's the inversion: **Arjuna accepts.** He picks up his bow. He fights. He fulfills his dharma (duty) because Krishna has shown him that the cosmic order requires it.

**Ahab refuses to be an instrument.** He doesn't want to be told that forces larger than himself are at work. He wants to strike through the mask. He wants to be the cause, not the effect. His entire obsession is about refusing the role Fedallah's prophecy assigns him.

And he dies exactly as prophesied anyway. Because — as Krishna told Arjuna — *with or without your involvement, these warriors are already slain.*

### Is Fedallah an Avatar?

No — not in the Hindu sense. He's a man. A broken, exiled, extraordinary man, but a man.

But in the novel's symbolic register, he occupies the avatar position: the figure who carries cosmic knowledge in a human body, who walks beside the warrior, who sees the ending while the warrior only sees the enemy.

The difference: Krishna is joyful. He loves Arjuna. He teaches him. He gives him the most complete vision of divine truth ever recorded in Hindu scripture. He does this out of love and cosmic duty.

Fedallah is silent. He doesn't teach Ahab. He doesn't explain. He gives him three cryptic prophecies and lets Ahab misinterpret them. If there is love in the Fedallah-Ahab bond, it is buried so deep that neither man can name it.

Krishna drives Arjuna to battle and the world is saved (dharma is restored). Fedallah drives Ahab to the whale and the ship goes down. Same structure. Opposite outcome. Because Ahab is not Arjuna. Ahab will not submit.

## Zoroastrianism and Hinduism: The Deep Connection

This is not a coincidence — these religions are cousins.

### Shared Origins

Both Zoroastrianism and Hinduism descend from the **Proto-Indo-Iranian** peoples who migrated from Central Asia around 2000 BCE. They split into two branches:
- The **Indic** branch moved southeast into India → Vedic religion → Hinduism
- The **Iranian** branch moved west into Persia → Zoroastrianism

They share a common ancestor language, common ancestor myths, and common ancestor gods.

### Cognate Terms

| Avestan (Zoroastrian) | Sanskrit (Hindu) | Meaning |
|----------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Asha** | **Rta** | Truth, cosmic order, righteousness |
| **Ahura** | **Asura** | Lord / divine being |
| **Haoma** | **Soma** | Sacred ritual plant/drink |
| **Mitra** | **Mitra** | Covenant, friendship, the sun |
| **Daeva** | **Deva** | — |
| **Navjote** | **Upanayana** | Sacred thread initiation ceremony |

### The Great Reversal

Here's what makes them cousins who became rivals:

- In Hinduism, **devas** are gods (good). **Asuras** are demons (bad).
- In Zoroastrianism, **Ahura** Mazda is God (good). **Daevas** are demons (bad).

The same words, reversed. What is divine in one tradition is demonic in the other. Scholars believe this reflects an ancient schism — the two branches splitting apart and each defining the other's gods as their own demons.

**Asha/Rta** — the most important cognate. Frashad's entire theology — his concept of cosmic order, truth, the structure of the universe — has a direct Sanskrit equivalent. When he walks through Hindu Bombay, the concept at the center of his religion has a name in the language spoken around him. He would recognize this. He would know that what he calls Asha, his Hindu neighbors call Rta.

### Ritual Parallels

- Both religions use sacred fire in worship
- Both have sacred thread ceremonies (Kusti / sacred thread)
- Both use a sacred ritual drink (Haoma / Soma)
- Both emphasize ritual purity
- Both have priestly castes with hereditary authority

## Would Frashad Know the Gita?

### His Education

A Mobed's son would receive formal priestly education:
- Trained at a **Herbedestan** (religious school for priests)
- Studied **Avestan** (the liturgical language — already dead by the 18th century, memorized phonetically)
- Studied **Pahlavi** (Middle Persian — the language of commentaries and translations)
- Studied **Zand** (interpretive commentary on the Avesta)
- Trained in the **Yasna** liturgy and ritual performance
- Underwent **navjote** (initiation) and **martab** (advanced consecration)

He would be literate. He would read and write Gujarati (the adopted language of the Parsee community). He would likely know some English (Bombay was a British trading center by the 1780s). He would have memorized extensive Avestan prayers without necessarily understanding all the words — the priests preserved the sounds across millennia.

### His Access to Hindu Texts

Bombay in the 1780s–1800s was a mixed city. Parsees, Hindus, Muslims, Christians, Jews, Armenians all lived in close proximity. The harbor was multilingual. The Parsees spoke Gujarati — a Hindu-majority language. They wore saris (adopted from Hindu custom). They lived alongside Hindu neighbors.

The Mahabharata and the Bhagavad Gita were oral traditions as much as written ones — stories told, retold, performed, sung. You didn't need to read Sanskrit to know the Gita. The stories of Krishna and Arjuna were part of the cultural atmosphere of western India. A bright, curious Mobed's son growing up in Bombay would almost certainly encounter them — in the harbor, in the market, from servants, from neighbors, from the general fabric of Gujarati-speaking society.

Would he read the Gita in Sanskrit? Unlikely — that would require specific Sanskrit education. But would he know the story of Krishna driving Arjuna to battle? Would he know the image of the god revealing his true form — the thousand mouths, the warriors already dead? Almost certainly. This was not an obscure text. It was the most famous story in India.

### What It Would Mean to Him

A Zoroastrian boy who has been taught about Asha (cosmic order) and Frashokereti (the final renovation) and the Saoshyant (the messiah who restores the world) encounters the Gita's teaching about dharma (duty), kala (time/death), and the avatar (god incarnate who descends to restore order).

He would recognize the architecture. Different names, different stories, but the same deep structure:
- The universe has an order
- That order is threatened
- A divine agent intervenes to restore it
- The human role is to participate in that restoration — not as the cause, but as the instrument

And then he would notice the difference: in the Gita, the instrument (Arjuna) submits. In Zoroastrian eschatology, the Saoshyant leads the final battle. But in both cases, the individual serves the cosmic order — not the other way around.

**This is what Ahab refuses.** Ahab will not be an instrument. He insists on being the cause. He strikes through the mask. And the universe answers exactly as Krishna said it would: *with or without your involvement, these warriors are already slain.*

## The White Whale and Vishnu

You asked: how does Vishnu relate to the white whale?

### The Whale as Cosmic Force

Moby Dick is not evil. He is not good. He is **inscrutable** — Ahab's word. He is a force of nature that Ahab insists on reading as a conscious enemy, but that may simply be the universe being the universe.

In the Gita framework, Moby Dick is closer to **kala** (Time) than to any god or demon. He is the force that destroys without malice, without intention, without caring whether Ahab understands or not. He is the thousand-mouthed form that consumes the warriors. He is the thing that was going to happen whether Ahab hunted it or not.

### Vishnu's First Avatar

Here's a detail worth noting: Vishnu's **first avatar** is **Matsya** — a fish. Specifically, a giant fish that saves the world from a primordial flood. The god takes the form of a sea creature to preserve creation.

Moby Dick is not an avatar of Vishnu. But the resonance is there: a vast creature in the ocean, white (purity? terror? blankness?), ancient, unkillable, carrying the world's meaning on its body. The whale contains something that Ahab can sense but never name. Krishna told Arjuna what it was. Moby Dick tells Ahab nothing.

### The Thousand Mouths

When Arjuna sees Krishna's true form, he sees **mouths consuming warriors**. When the Pequod's crew sees Moby Dick in the final chase, they see a vast white body that destroys boats, kills men, sinks the ship. The whale's jaw — the "crooked jaw" that Ahab describes when nailing the doubloon — is the mouth that consumes.

Frashad, standing in the bow of Ahab's boat, harpooner to the captain — if he knows the Gita, if he grew up hearing these stories in Bombay — what does he see when he looks at the white whale?

Does he see kala? Time? The force that has already determined the outcome?

Does he see the Vishvarupa — the cosmic form that contains all creation and all destruction simultaneously?

Or does he see what his own tradition would show him — Asha and Druj locked in their eternal battle, with the whale as the face of a universe that refuses to explain itself?

## Questions for the Novel

1. **Does Frashad think about the Gita?** In the long years at sea, in the quiet watches, does the story of the charioteer come back to him? Does he recognize himself in Krishna's role — the guide who drives the warrior to a battle that's already over?

2. **Does he see the parallel consciously or only feel it?** He might not think "I am Krishna" — that would be blasphemous in both traditions. But does he feel the shape of it? The chariot, the whaleboat. The battlefield, the ocean. The warrior who must be told that death is already decided.

3. **The critical difference:** Krishna reveals truth to save Arjuna. Fedallah reveals truth and it saves no one. Is this because Ahab won't listen? Or because Fedallah has stopped trying to save anyone — including himself?

4. **The Vishvarupa moment:** Is there a scene in the novel where Frashad sees something — the whale, the ocean, the fire — and perceives, even briefly, the cosmic form? The thing with no beginning and no end, the mouths consuming the warriors? And if so — does it terrify him, as it terrified Arjuna? Or has he been seeing it his whole life?

5. **Kala and Asha:** In the Gita, the cosmic force is called kala (Time). In Zoroastrianism, the cosmic order is called Asha (Truth). Are they the same thing? Is the force that kills everyone on the Pequod the same force that Frashad was trained since childhood to serve? If so — is his death an act of Druj (chaos) or Asha (order restored)?
