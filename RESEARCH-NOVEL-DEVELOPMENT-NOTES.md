# FRASHAD — Novel Development Notes

### Fedallah / Frashad: Historical & Narrative Architecture

---

## 1. Historical Grounding: Bombay 1810–1820

### The Qawasim — The Pirate Threat

In the early 19th century the East India Company organized a massive naval campaign against the **Qawasim tribal confederacy** — Arab pirates controlling the Arabian Gulf coast from the Qatar Peninsula to Oman. The EIC dispatched a first naval armada in **1809**, and a second major expedition sailed from **Bombay in November 1819**.

These were not distant pirates. They were attacking ships in the exact sea lanes between Bombay and the Persian Gulf. A Parsi merchant or ship's clerk on a trading dhow heading toward Muscat or Basra between 1812–1818 sailed directly through their hunting grounds.

### Parsis as Maritime Traders

By the early 19th century, Parsis had established lucrative trade with China (cotton and opium) and **dominated Bombay's shipbuilding industry**, enabling trade worldwide — including East Africa. Academic research specifically traces Parsi agency in the Western Indian Ocean region and East Africa, especially Mozambique, during exactly this period.

A young Parsi working on a trading vessel — as a supercargo, junior accountant, ship's interpreter — is not a stretch. It is almost the expected biography for a clever Zoroastrian youth of middling birth in Bombay circa 1810.

### The Indian Ocean Slave Pipeline

An Indian girl slave originally from Bombay ended up in Zanzibar after being sold by multiple men. The pipeline existed and moved people *from* Bombay outward. The EIC itself arranged slave voyages from Bombay and Madras to East and West Africa. Zanzibar, Mozambique, the Swahili Coast — all connected to Bombay by regular traffic.

### The Narrative Route

- **Capture**: Qawasim raiders take his vessel somewhere between Bombay and Muscat, ~1812–1815
- **First sale**: Arab slave market, possibly Oman or Aden
- **Second sale / transit**: East African coast — Zanzibar, Kilwa, Mozambique — where Parsi commercial networks also existed, creating the cruel irony that his own community's trade routes moved him
- **The ship in Africa**: By the time he surfaces on an African-connected vessel, he has passed through multiple hands — which is historically typical and gives time and transformation

## 2. The Name: Fadl Allah — فضل الله

**Fedallah** is almost certainly *Fadl Allah* — **"the grace/bounty of God."**

A Zoroastrian doesn't have to lie about this name. Ahura Mazda *is* God. The bounty of the Wise Lord is a real thing Frashad believes in. He is not taking a Muslim name — he is allowing Muslims to *hear* a Muslim name in sounds that mean something true to him. The deception lives entirely in the listener's interpretation.

This is not the Druj. This is the blade's edge of Asha.

## 3. The Disguise: Performance, Not Conversion

### What He Knew Before Capture

Parsi Bombay in 1810 was cosmopolitan. Large Muslim populations — Sunni merchants, Shia traders from Persia, Arab laskars on the docks. A smart, literate Parsi boy grew up hearing the *adhan* five times a day, knowing the basic *kalimah*, recognizing the gestures of salat. Islam was ambient, not studied. He didn't need to learn it. He just had to choose which parts of himself to make visible.

### He Performs — He Does Not Convert

**Full conversion would be spiritual death.** To speak the *shahada* sincerely would be to extinguish the inner flame. Frashad knows this. He will not do it.

**But performance is survival.** He learns the prayers. He says them at the right times, facing the right direction. He doesn't eat pork. He doesn't drink publicly. Total commitment to the surface. Complete separateness underneath.

The theological anchor he holds: *the fire doesn't go out just because you hide it. A covered flame is still a flame.*

What makes this agonizing: Zoroastrian moral framework doesn't have *taqiyya* (the Shia concept of permissible dissimulation). He is doing something his own tradition calls **Druj** — the Lie, the principle of Angra Mainyu — in order to preserve the self that serves Asha. He lives inside a paradox his theology gives him no clean resolution for.

That wound is permanent. It is still in him when Ahab finds him decades later.

### The Practical Shape of the Disguise

- He takes the name **Fadl Allah** at the **first point of sale**, not at capture. He chose it. It was an act of will, not defeat.
- He lets people assume he is a Persian Muslim from the Gulf coast — which explains any accent, any gaps in his Arabic, any strangeness in his practice.
- He is meticulous and cold. Not performing warmth or piety — just correctness. Other Muslims find him slightly unsettling without knowing why. He prays *exactly* right. Never more, never less.
- He never discusses theology. If pressed, he deflects with learning — quoting Rumi, Hafez — things a Persian Muslim would know that also carry Zoroastrian resonance only he can hear.
- **Inner life**: a daily recitation of the *Ashem Vohu* done silently, in the mind, before the Islamic fajr prayer. The Zoroastrian prayer hidden inside the Islamic one like a seed in stone.

## 4. The Theological Trap: *Majusi* — مجوسي

### The Word and Its Weaponization

*Majusi* is the Arabic term for Zoroastrian, derived from *Magu* — the Median priestly caste, the Magi. In the Quran it appears once, neutrally. But in Wahhabi and certain Sunni polemical tradition it became the dirtiest word in sectarian vocabulary — aimed specifically at **Shia Muslims**.

The logic of the slur:

- Shia reverence for Ali and Hussein = saint worship = idol worship
- Shia shrine visitation = pagan fire temple visitation
- The Shia calendar of mourning = Persian pre-Islamic ritual in disguise
- Persian ethnicity of many Shia = proof the conversion was never real

To a Wahhabi, the Shia weren't Muslims who erred. They were **Zoroastrians who never left.** Crypto-Majus. Fire worshippers hiding behind the shahada.

### What This Means for Frashad on a Wahhabi Ship

Being exposed as a Zoroastrian on a Qawasim-Wahhabi vessel is not just being a non-Muslim slave. It is being the **ur-enemy**. The original corruption. The thing Wahhabism defined itself *against* before it even turned on the Shia.

Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's reform project was explicitly framed as purging *Majusi* contamination from Islam. An actual living Majus on the ship is not a curiosity — he is an embodied theological catastrophe. A pollution.

A Shia captive might be treated with contempt but still recognized as technically Muslim, still carrying some protection. **Frashad carries none.** He is what the Shia are accused of being, made flesh.

### The Irony He Tastes Like Bile

Wahhabi Sunnis call Shia *Majusi* as the deepest insult available — and Frashad is the thing they are actually insulting. He is the referent of the slur. The Shia can at least protest the accusation. **He cannot.**

Further irony: Zoroastrian tradition is older than Islam by a millennium, shaped Persian culture, shaped Islamic civilization itself. The Zoroastrian concept of cosmic dualism filtered into Islamic angelology and eschatology. Even Wahhabi *tawhid* is a response to a religious landscape Zoroastrianism helped create.

They are calling the Shia by his name. His *real* name. And finding it the most monstrous thing they can say.

### His Interior Response

He doesn't just endure the slur. In his silent interior, he *corrects it* — not because Zoroastrianism is shameful, but because it is being used wrong, by people who don't know its depth, its age, its light. He carries that correction in silence for thirty years.

## 5. The Relationship with Ahab

### How Whaling Crews Actually Worked

This matters for understanding why Frashad's presence on the Pequod is so unusual.

**The lay system:** No one earned wages. Everyone — captain to greenhand — earned a percentage ("lay") of the voyage's total profit. Captain: ~1/8th. Harpooner: ~1/75th to 1/90th. Greenhand: ~1/350th. If the voyage was bad, everyone earned nothing.

**Voyage-by-voyage assembly:** Crews were assembled fresh for each voyage. Men signed shipping papers — a legal contract for that specific trip. When the voyage ended (2–4 years later), the oil was sold, lays were paid, and everyone dispersed. No permanent crews.

**Ships were owned by investors, not captains.** On the Pequod, the owners are Peleg and Bildad. They hired Ahab. A good captain could command the same ship multiple times — and Peleg says he "sailed as mate under Ahab" on a prior voyage, so Ahab has captained the Pequod before.

**Desertion was constant.** Conditions were brutal. Men deserted at every Pacific port — Lahaina, Honolulu, Hilo. Captains replaced deserters with local mariners. By the 1840s, 20% of New Bedford whaling crews were Pacific Islanders picked up mid-voyage.

**Harpooners were the exception.** Good harpooners were rare, valuable, and personally recruited by captains. A captain who found a great harpooner would seek him out for subsequent voyages. A harpooner of Frashad's caliber — "the Parsee who never misses" — would be exactly the kind of man a captain would track down and recruit again.

**What this means:** Frashad sailing with Ahab on a prior voyage is historically plausible and even expected. But Frashad being **smuggled aboard** the Pequod is not normal. That is a captain hiding a crewmember from his own ship's owners. That requires a reason the owners would refuse — and a bond strong enough to make Ahab defy them.

### What Melville Actually Shows Us

Ahab didn't hire Fedallah — he **smuggled** him. Hidden in the hold with his own crew of tiger-yellow men, below the knowledge of the ship's owners, below Starbuck, below everyone. This requires:

- Prior arrangement, made in secret, before the voyage
- Trust deep enough to stake the entire mission on
- A relationship the outside world cannot know about
- **A reason the owners (Peleg, Bildad) would not approve** — possibly because they know what happened on the prior voyage when Frashad was aboard and the whale took the leg

**Chapter 117**: They are clinging to a dead whale in the dark water together — one of the most intimate images in the novel — and Ahab says *tell me the prophecy **again**.* Not *what do you predict*. **Again.** He has heard it before. He needs to hear it again the way a man needs to hear a thing he already knows but cannot hold in the dark.

Ahab listens to Fedallah over Starbuck. That is not preference — that is allegiance to a different order of knowledge entirely.

### They Met Before. Long Before.

The Pequod is not the beginning of this relationship. It may be the end.

### BRANCH POINT: Was Frashad There When Ahab Lost His Leg?

The timeline allows it. Ahab lost his leg ~1836–1839. Frashad is in the Pacific by 1825, in Lahaina by 1830, known among captains as "the Parsee who never misses." If they met in the early 1830s, Frashad could have been Ahab's harpooner on the voyage where Moby Dick took the leg.

**Option A — Frashad was in the boat.**

He was there. He saw the whale rise. He saw the jaw come down. And here is the question that changes everything: **did he save Ahab's life?**

#### What Actually Happened to the Leg — The Medical Reality

Melville writes: *"suddenly sweeping his sickle-shaped lower jaw beneath him, Moby Dick had reaped away Ahab's leg, as a mower a blade of grass in the field."*

That is literature, not anatomy. **A sperm whale cannot cleanly sever a human leg.** The sperm whale's lower jaw is 16 feet long, narrow, V-shaped, with 18–26 conical peg-like teeth designed for gripping squid. These teeth are built for *holding*, not *cutting.* A sperm whale is not a shark. It doesn't bite through bone and muscle like a blade through grass.

What actually happened — what *always* happened in whaling — is the whale **stoved the boat.** The most common whaling accident. A sperm whale's head or tail hits a 25-foot wooden whaleboat and it shatters into timber. Men go into the water. In the wreckage, Ahab's leg was **crushed** — trapped between smashed planking and the whale's body, shattered by the impact, a compound fracture with bone through skin, the limb destroyed but still attached.

A crushed leg at sea is a death sentence — without intervention. Blood loss, shock, infection. The leg has to come off.

**Amputation at sea, 1830s:** American whaling ships were **not legally required to carry surgeons** (British ships were, since 1733). Many American whalers had no medical officer. The medicine chest contained a tourniquet, pliers, basic surgical tools, and numbered tinctures with no labels — just numbers keyed to a pamphlet most captains couldn't follow. No anesthesia. No antiseptic.

If a surgeon was present, he could complete the amputation in under two minutes — speed was survival. The tourniquet stops the bleeding, the knife cuts the flesh in a circular incision, the saw takes the bone, the flap is sutured closed. Then two weeks of prayer against infection.

If no surgeon was aboard, whoever had the steadiest hands and the strongest stomach did it. Documented case: **Captain Coffin**, so badly injured in a whaling accident that amputation was the only option, held a pistol to his mate's head and forced him to saw the leg off with a knife. Both men fainted when it was done.

#### What Frashad Did

If Frashad was in the boat — the harpooner, the man whose job puts him in the bow, closest to the whale — then when the boat shattered, he was in the water with Ahab. He is a man who has survived at sea for twenty years. He has been through worse than this. He does not panic.

He pulls Ahab from the wreckage. The leg is destroyed — crushed, bent wrong, bone visible, bleeding badly. Frashad gets a line around Ahab's thigh above the wound and twists it tight. A tourniquet improvised from hemp rope. He holds Ahab above water. He gets him onto the remaining boat or into the ship.

Then comes the question: **who cuts?**

If there is a surgeon, Frashad carries Ahab below and hands him over. But if there is no surgeon — and on many American whalers there was not — then someone else does it. The mate. The second mate. Or the harpooner who has been "extraordinary with a long blade" for his entire adult life. A man who has butchered whales, who has killed men, who knows how a blade moves through flesh and where bone resists.

Frashad may have amputated Ahab's leg.

Not metaphorically. With a knife, in a dim hold, with men holding Ahab down and Ahab screaming, and Frashad cutting as fast and clean as he can because speed is the only mercy available.

#### Zoroastrian Medicine — He Was Trained For This

This is not improvisation. The Vendidad (Fargard 7, verses 36–44) explicitly classifies three types of physician: one who heals with **herbs**, one who heals with the **Holy Word (manthra)**, and one who heals with **the knife**. Surgery — *karetô-baêshaza* — is a recognized and sanctioned medical practice in Zoroastrianism, stretching back to the legendary surgeon **Thrita**, who crafted the first surgical knife with gold-bound handles. The Sassanid-era **Academy of Jundishapur** was the world's first teaching hospital. The Islamic world's hospital system was built on this Zoroastrian-Persian foundation.

The Vendidad even prescribes a probationary system for surgeons: a physician must first practice on non-Zoroastrians. If his first three patients survive, he is qualified forever and "may at his will treat with the knife worshippers of Mazda, and heal them with the knife." If they die, he is forbidden to practice.

Frashad — son of a Mobed, raised in the priestly tradition, educated in the Avesta — would have known this. The knife-healer is not a violation of Zoroastrian law. It is one of the oldest roles in the tradition. A Mobed's son who became a healer-with-the-knife would be following a path the Vendidad itself authorizes.

And after twenty years at sea — cutting blubber, cutting line, killing whales, surviving violence — Frashad's hands are not a priest's hands anymore. They are a surgeon's hands. Steady, fast, precise. A man "extraordinary with a long blade" is also a man who knows where the blood runs and how bone resists.

The theological anchor: Zoroastrian purity law distinguishes between **living flesh** (Ahura Mazda's creation, to be healed) and **dead flesh** (nasu, inhabited by the Druj Nasu demon, to be avoided). Amputating a crushed leg from a living man is not handling nasu — it is *preventing* nasu. The knife that removes the dying limb serves Asha. The act of healing is the act of restoring cosmic order.

But the question Frashad carries afterward is not about the knife. It is about the *decision.* The Vendidad authorizes the cut. But did Asha authorize saving the man? Was Ahab supposed to die in that water? Did Frashad's knife restore order — or prevent the order from completing itself?

That is the wound he carries to the Pequod. Not guilt about the surgery. Guilt about the *rescue.*

#### The Cosmic Debt This Creates

If Frashad pulled Ahab from the water — if he stanched the bleeding, held him while the boat rowed back to the ship, kept him alive through the fever and the shock — then the cosmic debt Frashad spends the rest of the novel trying to repay is *a debt he created.* He saved a man who was supposed to die. He interfered with Asha. He kept the books from balancing.

If Frashad cut the leg off, the debt is even deeper. He didn't just save Ahab. **He made Ahab.** The man who walks on whale ivory, the man who bores a hole in the quarterdeck to brace his false leg, the man whose entire identity is organized around the missing limb — Frashad's blade created that man. Every step Ahab takes on the ivory leg is a step on something Frashad made possible.

The whale was the instrument of the order. The leg was the price. Ahab was supposed to pay with his life. And Frashad — the Zoroastrian, the man trained since birth to serve cosmic order — reached into the water and pulled him back. Out of instinct. Out of loyalty. Out of the bond between harpooner and captain. Not thinking about Asha. Just acting. A man saving another man.

And then, afterward — days later, weeks later, standing over Ahab's sweating body as the stump healed wrong and the fever burned — Frashad understands what he did. He disrupted the order. He saved a man the universe was trying to collect. He created the debt.

This makes the Pequod voyage not just Frashad's duty but his *correction.* He is not merely the instrument of Ahab's death. He is cleaning up his own mistake. He saved Ahab once. The universe has spent years reminding him that he wasn't supposed to. Now the whale is back, the debt is still open, and Frashad boards the Pequod knowing that this time he will not interfere. This time the order completes itself. This time he lets go.

His death on Day 2 is not just sacrifice. It is atonement. *I saved you once. I should not have. I am returning you now.*

And if he held the knife — if he cut the leg — then his death tangled in the whale line on Day 2 carries a specific symmetry: the hands that cut Ahab free are now caught in the very rope that binds Ahab to the whale. The instrument of salvation becomes the instrument of destruction. The line that should have been a lifeline becomes a death shroud.

**Option B — They met after the leg.**

Frashad found Ahab somewhere in the interregnum — after the whale, after the recovery, after the obsession took root. He saw a man walking on whale ivory and recognized the cosmic disruption. He did not create the debt. He inherited it. He was drawn to it the way a man trained to see disorder is drawn to the most disordered man in the Pacific.

This is cleaner but less devastating. Frashad's guilt is general — he serves the order — not specific. He is the charioteer, not the man who caused the accident.

**Option C — Combination.**

Frashad was on an earlier voyage with Ahab — not the one where the leg was lost, but one before it. They built the relationship over one or two prior voyages. Frashad saw Ahab whole. He knew the man before the whale broke him. Then Ahab sailed without Frashad and came back missing a leg.

Frashad sees the ivory and understands two things: the man he knew is gone, and the man who replaced him is a cosmic debt walking. He did not cause it, but he knew the man *before* — and that prior knowledge gives his role on the Pequod a different texture. He is not just the charioteer of a stranger. He is the charioteer of a man he once knew whole, now completing the destruction that began on a voyage Frashad wasn't there to prevent.

### What Melville Actually Tells Us About Who Knew Ahab Before

Melville leaves an enormous gap around the voyage where the leg was lost. Here is what the text *does* say:

**Captain Peleg** — co-owner of the Pequod, not sailing on it. He sailed as Ahab's mate on a prior voyage when a typhoon off Japan broke all three masts. He says: "I know Captain Ahab well; I've sailed with him as mate years ago; I know what he is — a good man." But this may or may not be the same voyage where the leg was taken. The typhoon could be a different trip entirely.

**Elijah** — the ragged, smallpox-scarred man on the dock. He calls Ahab "Old Thunder" and says "among some of us old sailor chaps, he goes by that name." He knows about a deathlike trance off Cape Horn, a "deadly skrimmage with the Spaniard afore the altar," and the loss of the leg. Most tellingly: "When Captain Ahab is all right, then this left arm of mine will be all right; not before." This implies Elijah was injured on the same voyage — possibly the same encounter — that cost Ahab his leg. **Elijah may be a former crewmate from the whale attack itself.**

**The three mates (Starbuck, Stubb, Flask)** — no prior voyages with Ahab mentioned. They appear to be newly assigned for the Pequod.

**The three harpooneers (Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo)** — Queequeg joins fresh in New Bedford with Ishmael. No prior connection to Ahab is stated for any of them.

**Fedallah and the "tiger-yellow" crew** — smuggled aboard secretly. Prior relationship clearly exists but its origin is never explained.

**The gap:** Melville tells us almost nothing about who was on the voyage where Moby Dick took the leg. Peleg may or may not have been there. Elijah hints at having been there. Everyone else on the Pequod appears to be new. The only person with an undeniable prior private relationship with Ahab is Fedallah — and Melville never explains when or how it began.

That gap is exactly where Frashad fits. If Frashad was Ahab's harpooner on the leg voyage, several things click into place:

- **Why Ahab trusts him absolutely** — Frashad was there for the worst moment of his life
- **Why Ahab smuggles him aboard** — they share a history no one else on the ship has access to
- **Why Ahab says "tell me again"** — Frashad has been telling him things for years
- **Why Fedallah stands behind Ahab at night on the quarterdeck** — the habit of standing watch together, formed on an earlier ship, continuing on this one
- **Why Elijah on the dock knows something dark** — Elijah was there too, saw Frashad, saw the bond form, and knows what it means that Ahab brought the Parsee back for another voyage

And if Elijah was also on that voyage — if Elijah *and* Frashad were both in the boat or on the ship when Moby Dick attacked — then the dockside scene in Chapter 19 gains another dimension: Elijah is warning Ishmael not just about Ahab, but about **the man Ahab has brought with him.** "Anything about your souls?" Elijah knows who is in the hold.

**Why Option A is the most powerful:**

Because it makes Frashad complicit. The novel asks: what does a man do when the cosmic order requires him to undo his own act of compassion? He saved Ahab's life — the most human, most instinctive, most *good* thing a man can do — and that act of goodness broke the universe. Now the universe needs him to let the same man die. To stand in the bow and not reach. To go into the water first, so his body can beckon Ahab after.

Every prophetic tradition Frashad has encountered tells the same story: you cannot outrun your duty. Jonah ran and was swallowed. Jeremiah tried to stop speaking and the words burned in his bones. Frashad saved a man's life and the universe spent years telling him he was wrong.

His last word — Asha — is not just "mission accomplished." It is "I have corrected my error." The order demanded a death. I delayed it. Now I am delivering it. The balance is restored. The fire is tended. I return to truth.

### Why Ahab Saw Through the Performance

Ahab is not Starbuck. He doesn't accept surfaces. He interrogates everything. At some point he looked at Fadl Allah and said something like:

*You pray like a man who learned the words from a book, not from his father.*

And the thirty years cracked. Not because Ahab was kind. Because he was **accurate.** Being finally seen correctly after three decades of the fold — even by a dangerous, half-mad whaling captain — was more than Frashad could hold against.

### What Frashad Offers That No Protestant Can

Protestant theology — Calvinist, Quaker, all of it — has **no satisfying account of active evil.** Satan is ultimately God's instrument. Everything bends to Providence. The whale, in Starbuck's world, is God's creature or God's test. Either way, you submit.

That is the answer Ahab **cannot live inside**.

Zoroastrianism is the only major world theology that says: **the darkness is real, it has agency, and fighting it is not sin — it is sacred duty.** Angra Mainyu is not God's instrument. He is the genuine opponent. The cosmic war is real. Resistance is not pride — it is righteousness.

For the first time since the whale took his leg, Ahab hears a framework in which his rage is not a spiritual failure. In which striking back at the thing that unmade you is not hubris but *Asha* — alignment with truth and light against the destructive principle.

Frashad didn't convert Ahab to Zoroastrianism. He gave Ahab the first theology that could **contain what Ahab already was**.

### The Moment of Telling

Not on the Pequod. Long before it.

It happens at night. No witnesses. After some exchange that has already gone further than either man intended — a conversation about fate, about what Ahab actually wants if he catches the whale.

Ahab reveals he doesn't want victory. He wants *contact*. He wants to put his hand on the thing that unmade him and force it to acknowledge him. He wants the universe to *answer*.

Frashad hears in this the oldest Zoroastrian question: is Angra Mainyu a real force, or just the absence of light? Is the darkness something, or just where the fire hasn't reached? Ahab is asking the question in a madman's language — but it is the same question.

Something in Frashad cracks open. Not dramatically. Not with tears. He doesn't have tears anymore after thirty years of the fold.

He says quietly, in the dark:

*My name is not Fadl Allah.*

He might not say *Zoroastrian*. He might say: *I am from before the Prophet. From before the caliphs. From fire.*

Ahab understands that. Ahab is also a man from before the categories that contain other men.

## 6. New England Protestantism and the Cosmic Taxonomy

### The Hierarchy of Souls, 1840s

Nantucket Quaker and Congregationalist New England had a specific theology of the non-Christian world — not just racism but a **cosmic taxonomy**:

- Catholics — wrong but Christian, if corrupted
- Jews — Old Testament, at least the root of truth
- Muslims — false prophet, active deception, spiritually dangerous
- Hindus — pagans, idol worshippers, pitiable
- Zoroastrians — **fire worshippers.** Barely a category. Almost pre-human in the Protestant imagination.

Fire worship to a Protestant is not just wrong theology. Fire is **Moloch**. It is the most visceral image of false religion in the entire Old Testament tradition. A Zoroastrian isn't just a heathen — he worships the thing that in Protestant imagination represents everything God forbade.

**And Ahab stakes his life on this man's prophecy.**

### Ahab Is Not Quite Protestant Anymore

Ahab was formed by Nantucket Quaker-Protestant culture — his bones, his language, his frame. But the whale *broke* that frame. The Protestant God did not show up.

By the time we meet Ahab he is still *speaking* Protestant — all that King James thunder in his sentences — but using that language **against** Protestant theology. He is a heretic who still speaks the heretic's native tongue.

*I'd strike the sun if it insulted me* — that is not a Christian statement. That is a man who has rejected the fundamental Protestant covenant: that God is sovereign and man submits.

Ahab has already left the building. He just hasn't found a new theology yet. Frashad is the new theology.

### What the Crew Sees

The other men see it. Starbuck sees it. These hidden dark men emerging from the hold disturb the crew at a level beneath ordinary racism. Not because they haven't seen Asian or African men. They've sailed everywhere. But Ahab's *personal* crew of tiger-yellow men signals something about Ahab they weren't prepared for:

**He has gone native in his soul.** He has placed his deepest trust not in a white Nantucket first mate but in a Parsi who worships fire and reads fate from a tradition older than Christ.

To the Pequod's crew, that is more alarming than the hunt itself. The captain has handed his inner life to a heathen.

## 7. The Emotional Core: Why Frashad Goes Down With Ahab

Ahab's willingness to hear Frashad is not respect in the normal sense. It is not *I see your full humanity.* It is *you carry something I need and I don't care what the world thinks of where you carry it.*

Frashad — who has been instrumentalized for thirty years, bought and sold and used — recognizes the difference between being valued as a person and being valued as a vessel of knowledge.

But: **it is still more than anyone has ever given him.**

A whaling captain from Nantucket who is too obsessed to be racist is still the first man who ever sat across from Frashad and said *tell me what you know* and meant it with his whole being.

Frashad knows the ship is doomed. He reads it clearly — not as prophecy necessarily, but as the reading of a man who has survived by reading situations for three decades. He has seen enough violence to know when something has moved past the point of return.

If the ship is already lost, the secret protects nothing anymore. The flame has been kept hidden to preserve the keeper — but if the keeper is already forfeit, what is the hiding for?

He goes down with Ahab not out of loyalty, not debt, not fatalism.

It is **grief.** He is going down with the only version of being known he ever got.

## 9. Frashad's Father — The Healer

### The Dual Role: Priest and Physician

In Zoroastrianism, the priestly and healing traditions are not separate tracks. They overlap and reinforce each other. The Vendidad (Fargard 7.44) ranks the **manthra-healer** — the one who heals with the Holy Word — as the highest form of physician. A Mobed who knows the sacred texts is, by definition, trained in the most potent form of healing the religion recognizes.

Frashad's father is Athornan — priestly family — but his primary practice is healing. He is the man the community calls when someone is sick, when a bone breaks, when a child won't stop coughing. He knows the manthra and he knows the knife. Frashad grows up in a house where the sacred fire burns *and* where people come bleeding, broken, sick.

### Three Possible Profiles for the Father

**Option A — The Mobed-Healer.** A practicing priest who is also the community healer. He tends the sacred fire *and* tends the sick. He recites manthra over the ill. He may also know herbs, bone-setting (the Parsi *hadvaid* tradition), and possibly even surgery. In smaller Parsi communities, the priest *was* the healer — the two roles were inseparable. This gives Frashad deep theological knowledge *and* exposure to medical practice from childhood.

**Option B — The Athornan Physician.** He comes from a priestly family but chose medicine over full-time temple service. Not all men from Athornan families became practicing Mobeds — the tradition allowed them to pursue other professions while retaining priestly lineage (the right to enter priesthood could be exercised up to the fifth generation). His daily work is healing. He is a knife-healer or herb-healer who carries the priestly lineage without wearing the priestly robes every day. This explains how Frashad has deep theological knowledge *and* practical medical training.

**Option C — The Hadvaid (Bone-Doctor).** The Parsi tradition has a specific healing role: the *hadvaid* — literally "bone-doctor" (had = bone, vaid = doctor). A gifted healer who diagnoses disorders of bones and nerves through highly developed touch, manipulates joints and vertebrae, applies poultices. Deep anatomical knowledge passed from father to son. If Frashad grew up watching his father set bones, feel for fractures, manipulate spines — then when Ahab's leg is crushed, Frashad doesn't just have a steady hand. He has a *trained* hand. He knows where the bone breaks clean and where it splinters. He can feel the fracture through the flesh and know: *this leg is dead. It has to come off.* And the word *hadvaid* — bone-doctor — carries its own terrible irony. The man who was raised to heal bones is the man who has to cut through one.

### The Narrative Function

If the father is a healer, Frashad's entire childhood is reframed. He doesn't grow up in a purely religious household. He grows up in a house where theology and medicine are the same discipline — where healing and holiness are the same act — where restoring a broken body is restoring Asha, putting the world back in order.

This education — theological *and* medical — is exactly what makes him the man who can amputate Ahab's leg at sea and then spend years wondering whether he should have. The knife was clean. The surgery was sanctioned by the Vendidad itself. But was the *decision* to save the man aligned with Asha — or a disruption of it?

### Connection to the Parsi-British Medical World

The Wadia family — Parsi shipbuilders in Bombay — built over 100 warships for Britain, including **HMS Minden** (1810) and **HMS Trincomalee** (1817, the oldest surviving British warship). Parsis were not marginalized laborers in Bombay. They *built* the British navy's Indian fleet.

A Parsi healer's son growing up in this Bombay — where his community builds the ships, trades with China, and is integrated into British colonial infrastructure — would have been exposed to British naval medicine as naturally as to his father's practice. He would see British surgeons arriving on the ships his community built. He would hear about amputation techniques from Napoleonic Wars veterans circulating through Bombay's docks.

### THE STAR-SPANGLED BANNER WAS WRITTEN ON A ZOROASTRIAN-BUILT SHIP

**HMS Minden** — a 74-gun British warship — was launched from Bombay on June 19, 1810. It was built of Indian teak by Parsi master shipbuilder **Jamsetjee Bomanjee Wadia** at the Duncan Docks.

On September 14, 1814, during the British bombardment of Fort McHenry in Baltimore, **Francis Scott Key** wrote the poem that became **"The Star-Spangled Banner"** — the American national anthem — while aboard or in sight of this ship.

The American national anthem was written on a ship built by Zoroastrians.

Frashad is 27 years old when HMS Minden launches from the docks of his home city, built by men from his community. The same community that will produce no record of his existence. The same docks he may have walked past as a boy, watching his people build the instruments of an empire that does not know their names.

The connections between Bombay, the British navy, and America run deeper than anyone remembers. A Parsi built the ship. An American wrote the anthem. A Zoroastrian named Frashad will sail on an American whaler and die on an American captain's obsession. None of these threads are visible from the surface. They are all true.

## 10. Maritime Surgery and the British at Bombay — Historical Context

### Surgery at Sea: A Timeline

| Year | Development | Relevance |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| 1718 | Jean Louis Petit develops the screw-compressor **tourniquet** — "the most important surgical advancement before anesthesia" | The tool that saves Ahab's life |
| 1733 | Britain requires all whaling ships to carry **surgeons** (British law; American whalers were exempt and often sailed without) | British ships at Bombay have surgeons; American whalers often don't |
| 1793–1815 | **Napoleonic Wars** — massive development in naval surgery; Dominique Jean Larrey revolutionizes field amputation, invents the "flying ambulance," develops triage | Surgeons trained in these wars cycle through Bombay |
| ~1800 | A competent naval surgeon can amputate in **under 2 minutes**; French surgeon Lisfranc amputates at the thigh in 10 seconds; Benjamin Bell cuts through all but the bone in 6 seconds | Speed is survival without anesthesia |
| 1805 | Royal Navy renames "surgeon's mate" to **"assistant surgeon"** — the medically trained assistant who visits patients, dresses wounds, maintains instruments | A role a trained Parsi could plausibly fill |
| 1810 | **HMS Minden** launched from Bombay — built by Parsi shipbuilder Jamsetjee Bomanjee Wadia. 74-gun ship of the line, crewed and provisioned in Bombay | Parsis build British warships; British naval surgeons serve on Parsi-built ships |
| 1817 | **HMS Trincomalee** built in Bombay by Wadia family — now the oldest surviving British warship | The integration is deep and ongoing |
| 1826 | EIC establishes first **Native Medical School** in Bombay to train Indians in modern medicine (fails by 1832) | The British *wanted* trained Indian medical personnel |
| 1842 | **Ether anesthesia** first demonstrated (Crawford Long); widespread use after 1846 | Too late for Ahab's amputation (~1836–1839) |
| 1845 | **Grant Medical College** founded in Bombay by Jagannath Shankarseth and Parsi philanthropist **Sir Jamsetjee Jeejeebhoy** | Parsis co-found Bombay's major medical institution |

### Would a British Ship Hire a Parsi Apprentice Surgeon?

**Short answer: not formally, but practically — yes.**

The Royal Navy's surgeon's mate (renamed "assistant surgeon" in 1805) was a trained medical assistant who dressed wounds, visited patients in sick bay, prepared medicines, and maintained instruments. This was a well-paid position — £9 2s per month in 1815, equivalent to a lieutenant's pay.

**Formally:** The Royal Navy would not commission an Indian as a surgeon's mate. The rank was reserved for British subjects who had passed examination. The EIC's medical service (Bombay Medical Service) employed British surgeons exclusively in covenanted positions.

**But practically:** Ships operating out of Bombay needed medical support. The EIC itself established a Native Medical School in Bombay in 1826 specifically because they *wanted* trained Indian medical personnel — the school failed, but the impulse was there. And below the formal rank structure, ships carried "dressers," "loblolly boys," and unnamed assistants who helped the surgeon. On an EIC merchant vessel or a Bombay-based trading ship, a Parsi youth from a healing family who could stitch wounds, set bones, and prepare medicines would be valuable regardless of whether anyone called him "surgeon's mate."

**The more plausible path for Frashad:** He doesn't serve on a British warship. He serves on a **Parsi-owned trading vessel** — a dhow or merchantman sailing between Bombay, Muscat, and the Persian Gulf. These ships would not carry a British surgeon. They would carry whatever medical knowledge the crew brought with them. A healer's son — trained in the hadvaid tradition, knowing herbs, knowing the knife — would be the closest thing to a doctor on board.

This is the ship the Qawasim capture.

### Would Arabian Pirates Attack British Ships?

**Absolutely — they did, repeatedly, and it triggered two wars.**

The **Qawasim tribal confederacy** attacked British-flagged and British-associated vessels throughout the early 19th century:

- **Pre-1809:** Escalating attacks on EIC shipping in the Persian Gulf, compounded by French diplomatic missions to Muscat and Tehran (1807) that threatened British interests
- **1809 campaign:** British naval expedition from Bombay destroys three Qawasim bases and 80+ vessels, but fails to permanently suppress them. Attacks resume by 1811
- **1815:** After a peace treaty broke down, Qawasim "indulged in a carnival of maritime lawlessness":
  - Capture and murder of most of a British Indian vessel's crew near Muscat
  - January 6: capture of armed pattamar *Deriah Dowlut*, 17 of 38 Indian crew murdered
  - 1816: three British-flagged merchant vessels from Surat taken, most crews killed
- **November 1819:** Second major British expedition sails from Bombay, attacks Ras Al Khaimah (December 3–9), deposes the Qawasim leader, leads to the General Maritime Treaty of 1820

The victims were not only British warships. The Qawasim attacked **merchant vessels, trading dhows, and Indian-crewed ships flying British colors.** A Parsi-owned trading vessel sailing from Bombay toward Muscat or Basra between 1812 and 1818 sailed directly through Qawasim hunting grounds. These were the exact sea lanes the Qawasim controlled.

**For the novel:** Frashad's capture doesn't require being on a British warship. A Parsi trading dhow — the kind of vessel his community routinely sailed — is a natural target. The Qawasim made no distinction between British warships and Indian merchant vessels flying under British protection. They attacked everything.

## 11. The Healer Who Kills Whales — The Corruption of Frashad

### The Theological Devastation

Frashad's father was a healer. His religion's oldest hymn — Yasna 29, attributed directly to Zarathustra — is **"The Cow's Lament"**: the Soul of the Cow (Geush Urvan, representing all animal life) crying out to Ahura Mazda: *"Slaughter without measure has made my life full of sorrow. For whom was I created? Who will protect me?"* God's answer: Zarathustra will protect you.

Frashad grew up hearing this hymn. The animal's cry to God for protection from slaughter. The promise that the righteous will answer that cry.

And now he kills whales. The largest animal on earth. He answers the cry with a harpoon.

### The Kar-Fish — What Frashad Is Really Killing

In the Bundahishn (the Zoroastrian creation text), Ahura Mazda created the **Kar-Fish** — the greatest of all water creatures, chief of everything that lives in the sea. Ten Kar-Fish circle the Tree of Life, protecting it from the forces of Angra Mainyu. One — the **Vas of Panchasadvaran** — is so enormous its length is described as *"what a man will walk from dawn till the sun goes down."* A creature the size of a day's journey. With the keenest eyesight of all living things.

A whale-sized guardian of cosmic order, created by Ahura Mazda to protect the Tree of Life until Frashokereti.

And Frashad hunts whales.

Every sperm whale he kills could be, in the deepest layer of his theology, a relative of the Kar-Fish. A guardian creature of the good creation. The son of a healer — the man raised on Geush Urvan's cry — who now slaughters the ocean's largest creatures for the profit of Nantucket shipowners.

### The Layers of Corruption

The fall is not sudden. It is incremental. Each step takes him further from what he was:

1. **The boy** — son of a healer, tending the fire, learning Yasna 29, reciting the Ashem Vohu
2. **The exile** — expelled, fleeing, but still carrying the prayers, still keeping the inner flame
3. **The performer** — taking the name Fadl Allah, praying toward Mecca, living inside the Druj to preserve Asha
4. **The sailor** — working dhows, using his father's healing knowledge to survive, still refusing to eat certain things, still reciting the prayers in his mind
5. **The whaler** — standing in the bow with a harpoon, killing the largest creatures God ever made, creatures that in his theology are kin to the Kar-Fish, guardian of the Tree of Life
6. **The killer** — "he never misses." Known among captains for his accuracy. Valued for his skill at ending the lives of things that are, by his own tradition, sacred

And the final irony: the whale that takes Ahab's leg — the creature that Ahab calls his enemy — may be, in Frashad's private theology, *an instrument of Asha.* The whale is not Angra Mainyu. The whale is the Kar-Fish defending the order. Ahab attacked it. The whale answered. And Frashad — the healer's son who kills whales — saved the man the whale was trying to collect.

He has violated every layer of his calling. And he knows it.

### Zoroastrian Animals — Not Like Hinduism

Zoroastrianism is not vegetarian. Parsi cuisine is heavily meat-focused. The religion divides animals into **beneficent** (cattle, dogs — creations of Ahura Mazda, to be protected) and **khrafstra** (snakes, rats, scorpions — creations of Angra Mainyu, to be killed as sacred duty). There was an annual Feast of Pest Killing.

But whales fit neither category. They are not domestic cattle. They are not noxious vermin. They are the largest creatures in the ocean — and the Kar-Fish, chief of all water creatures, is their cosmic analogue. Frashad cannot classify what he is doing. His theology has no clean box for "hunting the greatest creature in the sea for profit." It is not khrafstra-killing (sacred duty). It is not cattle-slaughter (forbidden). It is something the Avesta never imagined because no Zoroastrian ever stood in the bow of a whaleboat before.

He is doing something his religion has no word for. That silence — the absence of a category — may be the loneliest thing about his life at sea.
