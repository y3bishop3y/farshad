# Prophecy — Research Reference

*Compiled for FRASHAD: A Novel*

*How different traditions treat prophets, and why it matters that Moby-Dick has three prophetic traditions colliding on the same ship.*

---

## The Three Prophets of Moby-Dick

The Pequod carries (at minimum) three distinct prophetic traditions aboard, and encounters a fourth on the dock before sailing:

| Prophet | Tradition | What He Prophesies | How He's Received |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Elijah** (Ch. 19) | Jewish/Christian (Biblical parallel) | Warns Ishmael about Ahab — "anything about your souls?" | Dismissed as a humbug, a crazy man |
| **Fedallah** (Ch. 117) | Zoroastrian (Parsee) | Three prophecies: two hearses, he dies first, hemp kills Ahab | Feared as the devil, an evil angel |
| **Pip** (Ch. 93+) | African-American / divine madness | "Saw God's foot upon the treadle of the loom, and spoke it" | Called insane, an idiot |
| **Ahab** (Ch. 36+) | Self-appointed / Promethean | Declares the whale must die — imposes his will as cosmic law | Obeyed, followed to death |

Four men who see something the others can't. Each one is rejected, feared, or dismissed. The novel is asking: what do you do when someone tells you the truth and you can't hear it?

## Elijah in Moby-Dick (Chapter 19: "The Prophet")

### What He Actually Says

A ragged, smallpox-scarred man accosts Ishmael and Queequeg on the Nantucket wharf after they sign aboard the Pequod:

> "Shipmates, have ye shipped in that ship?"

> "Anything down there about your souls?"

> "A soul's a sort of a fifth wheel to a wagon."

He calls Ahab **"Old Thunder"** and alludes to dark events in Ahab's past:
- A deathlike trance lasting three days off Cape Horn
- "That deadly skrimmage with the Spaniard afore the altar"
- A mysterious silver calabash
- The loss of his leg — "according to the prophecy"

He never makes a specific prediction. He simply warns. He is asking: do you know what you've signed up for?

### Ishmael's Response

Ishmael calls him "a humbug" and dismisses him — but notices Elijah following them afterward and can't quite shake the unease. The rational mind rejects what the gut recognizes.

### The Biblical Parallel

Melville named him Elijah deliberately. In 1 Kings, the prophet Elijah confronts King Ahab of Israel — a wicked king married to Jezebel who has led Israel into the worship of Baal (a foreign god).

**The Naboth story (1 Kings 21):** King Ahab wants Naboth's vineyard. Naboth refuses to sell his ancestral land. Jezebel arranges Naboth's murder through false witnesses. Ahab takes the vineyard. God sends Elijah to confront him:

> "Thus says the LORD: In the place where dogs licked the blood of Naboth, dogs will lick your blood, even yours."

Ahab repents — tears his clothes, wears sackcloth, fasts. God delays the punishment to Ahab's sons' generation. But the prophecy holds. Ahab dies in battle, struck by a random arrow. Dogs lick his blood from his chariot.

**The key parallel:** In both the Bible and Moby-Dick, a prophet confronts an Ahab and warns him. In both cases, the Ahab has been warned and continues anyway. In both cases, the prophecy is fulfilled exactly.

## Jewish Prophecy — The Navi Tradition

This is the tradition Melville is drawing from when he names his dockside prophet "Elijah." And you're right — it works fundamentally differently from Zoroastrian prophecy.

### Prophets Are Honored, Not Stigmatized

In Judaism, a prophet (**navi**) is:

- "A spokesman for God, a person chosen by God to speak to people on God's behalf"
- "Role models of holiness, scholarship, and closeness to God"
- People who "set the standards for the entire community"

The Talmud teaches there were **hundreds of thousands** of prophets throughout Jewish history. Tradition names **48 prophets and 7 prophetesses** as canonical — but prophecy itself is widespread, expected, and valued.

### How Prophets Are Chosen

This is the critical difference from Zoroastrianism:

- A prophet is NOT chosen by the priesthood
- A prophet is NOT authorized by a religious institution
- A prophet's authority comes **"entirely from the fact that God chose them to be His messengers"**
- Prophecy is described as "the culmination of a person's spiritual and ethical development" — when someone reaches sufficient spiritual depth, "the Shechinah (Divine Spirit) comes to rest upon him or her"

Prophets came from everywhere:
- Moses — raised as an Egyptian prince
- Amos — a herdsman
- Elisha — a plowman
- Daniel — a government administrator
- Jeremiah and Ezekiel — priests (but their prophetic authority was separate from their priestly role)

**No one authorizes a Jewish prophet.** God does. The community can reject a prophet, ignore a prophet, even kill a prophet — but they cannot un-authorize one. The prophet's legitimacy comes from above, not from the institution.

### Samuel and Saul — A Prophet Chooses a King

You brought this up and it's exactly right. In 1 Samuel 10, the prophet Samuel anoints Saul as the first king of Israel. A prophet — not a priest, not an elder council, not a military leader — picks the king. Samuel pours oil on Saul's head and says:

> "Has not the Lord anointed you to be prince over his people Israel?"

Then Samuel prophesies that the Spirit of God will come upon Saul and Saul himself will prophesy with a band of prophets. It happens. The people ask: "Is Saul also among the prophets?"

The point: in Judaism, prophetic authority outranks royal authority. A prophet can make a king. A prophet can also unmake one — as Samuel later rejects Saul and anoints David instead. The prophet answers to God, not to the throne, not to the temple.

### The Prophet vs. The Priest

Judaism recognizes two forms of religious leadership:

- **Kohen** (priest) — hereditary, institutional, maintains the Temple, performs sacrifices, follows established ritual
- **Navi** (prophet) — called by God, often outside the establishment, speaks uncomfortable truths, challenges power

These two roles are separate. Sometimes they overlap (Jeremiah was both), but the prophetic calling is NOT a subset of the priestly one. A prophet can be anyone. A priest must be born to it.

**This is the opposite of Zoroastrianism,** where prophetic/spiritual authority flows through the priestly class. In Judaism, God can call a shepherd out of a field and make him speak for the Almighty. In Zoroastrianism, if that shepherd claims to hear God's voice, he's a yatu.

## Zoroastrian Prophecy — The Closed System

### Who Authorizes Prophecy

In Zoroastrianism, legitimate divine communication flows through a specific structure:

1. **Zarathustra** received the original revelation from Ahura Mazda
2. That revelation was codified in the **Avesta**
3. The **priesthood** (Mobeds, Herbads, Dasturs) preserves and interprets the Avesta
4. The **Saoshyant** (messiah figure) will appear at the appointed time in the cosmic calendar to bring the final renovation

There is no provision for freelance prophets. The revelation happened. It was recorded. The priests guard it. The next legitimate divine intervention is the Saoshyant, and that will happen on a cosmic timetable — not because some farmer's son in Khorasan had a dream.

### The Cosmic Calendar

Zoroastrians believe in a **12,000-year world cycle**:
- Zarathustra appeared at the end of the 9th millennium
- A Saoshyant appears at the end of each subsequent millennium
- The final Saoshyant brings Frashokereti — the renovation of the universe

When Behāfarīd claimed prophecy in 747 CE, the priests checked the calendar: **it wasn't time.** Therefore he was not the Saoshyant. Therefore his visions were not from Ahura Mazda. Therefore they were from Angra Mainyu. Therefore he was a yatu (sorcerer). Therefore he had to die.

The logic is airtight within the system. There is no room for an unauthorized prophet.

### What Happened to Behāfarīd

**Who:** A Zoroastrian man from Zūzan, Khorasan (northeastern Iran). Son of a man named Farvardīn (a Zoroastrian name). Hometown had a fire temple. He was one of them.

**When:** Active ~745–749 CE. Executed 748 or 749 CE.

**What he claimed:** He disappeared for seven years (allegedly to China). Came back claiming divine revelation. Staged a "resurrection" using a green silk shirt from China. Said it was a heavenly garment. Wrote a book in Persian laying out his teachings. Attracted followers fast — mostly poor Zoroastrian farmers.

**What he actually taught:**
- Accepted Zarathustra as a true prophet
- Prescribed seven daily prayers facing the sun (instead of five)
- Banned wine and intoxicants
- Banned consanguineous marriage (which orthodox Zoroastrianism permitted)
- Restricted animal slaughter — only old or weak animals could be killed
- Required a tithe of one-seventh of wealth for public works (bridges, roads)
- Called for helping the sick and needy

Most of this is compassionate reform. Some of it borrows from Islam. Scholars see it as "at root social in nature" — a reform movement for disenfranchised Zoroastrian peasants.

**Why the priests wanted him dead:** He claimed prophetic authority. He claimed to have received revelation. He wrote scripture. He was not a priest. He was not the Saoshyant. The cosmic calendar said it wasn't time. Therefore: heresy.

But there was also politics. He was pulling followers away from the established priesthood. He was challenging their monopoly on religious truth. He was offering poor people a simpler, more accessible form of worship. The priests saw an existential threat.

**How he died:** The Zoroastrian priests — the **Mobeds and Herbads** — went to **Abu Muslim**, the Muslim Abbasid general who was conquering Khorasan. Their own conqueror. Their oppressor. And they said: *Behāfarīd is destroying both Islam AND Zoroastrianism. Kill him.*

Abu Muslim sent an army. Behāfarīd was captured in the mountains. Dragged to Nīshāpūr. They offered him a chance to convert to Islam. He accepted — but they hanged him anyway, saying his conversion wasn't sincere. Hanged at the Bāb al-Jāmeʿ (the gate of the Friday Mosque). Many followers also executed.

His followers said he rode a golden horse to heaven and would return.

**The bottom line:** The Zoroastrian religious establishment handed one of their own people to the Muslim authorities to be killed because he claimed to speak for God outside their sanctioned channels. The content of his message didn't matter. The source was wrong.

## Islamic Prophecy — The Sealed Tradition

Frashad moves through the Muslim world for decades. He takes an Arabic name. He would have encountered this framework constantly.

### Muhammad as the Seal

Islam recognizes a long line of prophets — Adam, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and many others are all prophets (**nabi**) in Islamic tradition. But Muhammad is the **Khatam al-Nabiyyin** — the "Seal of the Prophets." Quran 33:40:

> "Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but he is the Messenger of Allah, and the Seal of the Prophets."

This means: **prophecy is over.** The final message has been delivered. The Quran is complete. No new prophet will come. After Muhammad, only caliphs (political successors), not prophets (divine messengers).

Muhammad's own metaphor: He compared himself to the last brick in a building. All the other bricks (prophets) came before. He is the one that completes the structure. The building is finished. No more bricks are needed.

### What This Means for Frashad

In the Muslim world he inhabits for decades (Zanzibar, Muscat, the dhow routes), anyone claiming prophetic vision would be:
- At best, a Sufi mystic — tolerated in some circles, persecuted in others
- At worst, a false prophet — a direct challenge to Muhammad's finality
- If he's Zoroastrian claiming Zoroastrian prophecy, he's a dhimmi having visions — irrelevant to Islam, but potentially dangerous

Frashad learns to keep his mouth shut. The Muslim world doesn't care about his Zoroastrian visions. But it also won't protect him if he speaks them aloud.

## Christian Prophecy — The Witch Problem

The Pequod's crew is mostly Christian — New England Protestants, Nantucket Quakers, and sailors from various backgrounds. This matters because the Christian tradition's relationship to prophecy is the most anxious and violent of all.

### The Split

Christianity inherits the Jewish prophetic tradition but develops a deep anxiety about it:

- The **Old Testament prophets** are honored — Elijah, Jeremiah, Isaiah are heroes of the faith
- But after Christ, the status of prophecy becomes contested
- **False prophets** become a central fear — Jesus himself warns "Beware of false prophets, who come to you in sheep's clothing" (Matthew 7:15)
- By the medieval period, unauthorized spiritual experience is increasingly dangerous

### From Prophet to Witch

The European witch trials (1450–1750) represent the extreme endpoint of Christian anxiety about unauthorized spiritual power:
- ~100,000–110,000 people prosecuted
- ~40,000–60,000 executed
- 80% were women over 40
- The accusations mirrored earlier charges against Jews and heretics: devil-worship, cannibalism, sexual deviance

The Dominican Inquisition (founded 1233) treated **magic and heresy as two sides of the same offense.** If your spiritual experience didn't come through the Church, it came through the Devil. Sound familiar?

### The Nantucket Quaker Angle

Nantucket was a Quaker community. This adds another layer:

- Quakers believe in the **"Inner Light"** — the spirit of God dwelling within each person
- No priests, no ministers, no intermediaries between the individual and God
- Worship is silent. Anyone can speak when moved by the Spirit.
- This is the most prophetically democratic tradition in Christianity — anyone can receive divine communication

Captain Peleg and Captain Bildad (the Pequod's owners) are Quakers. Starbuck is likely from a Quaker family (the Starbucks were a prominent Nantucket Quaker clan). They come from a tradition that, in theory, allows for individual prophetic experience.

But they still call Fedallah the devil.

## The Collision on the Pequod

Here's what's actually happening on Melville's ship, prophetically speaking:

**Elijah** (Jewish tradition) → Prophecy as warning. The prophet stands outside the power structure, speaks truth to power, and walks away. He's called crazy but he's not punished. His tradition says: *a prophet is honored, even when rejected.*

**Fedallah** (Zoroastrian tradition) → Prophecy as doom. He already knows what will happen. He doesn't try to change it. He walks into it. His tradition expelled him for this gift — or curse — and the Pequod's crew fears him for the same reason his community did. His tradition says: *if we didn't authorize it, it's from the enemy.*

**Pip** (African-American / the ocean as revelation) → Prophecy as madness. He saw something real — "God's foot upon the treadle of the loom" — and it destroyed his mind. His prophetic experience is dismissed as insanity. His tradition (if he has one — he's a Black boy from Connecticut in the 1840s, possibly descended from slaves) gives him no framework for what happened to him.

**Ahab** (self-appointed / Promethean) → Prophecy as will. Ahab doesn't receive visions — he *imposes* them. He decides the whale must die and bends the entire ship to his vision. He is the anti-prophet: instead of speaking for God, he speaks *against* God. Instead of receiving truth, he manufactures it.

### The Question for Frashad

Frashad/Fedallah is the only prophet on the ship whose tradition actively punished him for his gift. Elijah walks free. Pip is pitied. Ahab is obeyed. Fedallah is feared.

He has moved through three religious worlds:
1. **Zoroastrian Bombay** — where his visions made him a yatu (sorcerer)
2. **The Muslim Indian Ocean** — where his visions were irrelevant (prophecy is sealed, no new prophets)
3. **The Christian/Quaker Pequod** — where his visions make him the devil

In every world, the same gift produces a different form of exile. The content of what he sees doesn't change. Only the framework changes. And in every framework, the answer is the same: *we don't want to hear it.*

## Open Questions

- Does Frashad understand these different frameworks? Has he learned, over decades, that his gift will be received differently depending on who's listening — and that it will be rejected everywhere?
- Does he eventually stop caring how it's received? Does he reach a point where he simply speaks what he sees, without expectation?
- When he delivers the three prophecies to Ahab, is he performing the role of prophet for the first time since Bombay? Is it a relief? A return?
- Does he see the parallel between himself and Elijah on the dock? Two prophets, two traditions, both warning about the same doom — and both ignored?
- What does Pip's madness/vision look like to Fedallah? Does he recognize it? Does he see in Pip someone who received the gift without training, without any framework at all — and was destroyed by it?
