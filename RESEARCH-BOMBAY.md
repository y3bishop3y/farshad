# Bombay — The World Frashad Was Born Into (1661–1820)

*Research for FRASHAD: A Novel*

---

## The Name

The city Frashad knew was called **Bombay** — derived from the Portuguese "Bombaim," which is commonly explained as "Bom Bahia" ("good bay"), though the more likely origin is the local name **Mumbadevi**, after the Hindu goddess Mumba, the city's patron deity.

Marathi and Gujarati speakers always called it Mumbai. The British called it Bombay. In 1995, the Shiv Sena government officially renamed it Mumbai, arguing "Bombay" was a colonial corruption. But in the 1780s–1810s, the period we care about, it is Bombay — a city of seven islands, not yet connected by the land reclamation projects that would later fuse them into a single peninsula.

---

## How the British Got It — A Wedding Gift (1661)

Bombay did not fall to British conquest. It was a **dowry payment.**

In 1534, Sultan Bahadur Shah of Gujarat ceded the seven-island archipelago to Portugal under the Treaty of Bassein. For 127 years, the Portuguese held it — a minor colonial outpost focused on Christian conversion, with limited trade development.

In 1661, **Catherine of Braganza**, sister of the King of Portugal, married King **Charles II** of England. The marriage treaty (June 23, 1661) transferred Bombay to Charles along with the port of Tangier, trading privileges, and approximately £300,000 in Portuguese crowns. A secret article obligated England to provide military support to Portugal against the Dutch and Spanish in India.

Charles regarded Bombay as a liability. On **March 27, 1668**, he leased it to the **East India Company** for **£10 per year.** The EIC would hold it for two centuries.

Governor Gerald Aungier (1669–1677) transformed it — establishing a mint, a printing press, courts, and encouraging migration from the mainland. He invited artisans, weavers, merchants, and traders of all religions to settle. **This is when the Parsis came in significant numbers.**

## The City Frashad Was Born Into (~1783)

### The Physical Place

In the 1780s, Bombay was a fortified colonial settlement on a cluster of islands. The Fort — the British administrative center — had three main gates: **Apollo Gate, Church Gate, and Bazaar Gate.** Fort George had been constructed in 1769. Batteries and bastions protected the harbor.

Outside the Fort walls lay the "native town" — dense, crowded, polyglot. Hindu temples, Muslim mosques, Parsi fire temples, Jewish synagogues, Portuguese Catholic churches — all within walking distance. The harbor was thick with ships: EIC merchantmen, Parsi-built warships, Arab dhows, fishing boats.

### The Population

| Year | Total Population | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| 1780 | ~100,000 | Parsis = 9.2% (~9,200) |
| 1790 | ~120,000+ | Gujarat famine drives massive Parsi migration to Bombay |
| 1812 | ~235,000 | Fixed: 165,000; migratory: 50,000; famine refugees: 20,000. Parsi population has **quadrupled** since 1780 |

The population was overwhelmingly **Hindu**, with large **Muslim** communities (Sunni merchants, Shia traders from Persia, Arab lascars on the docks), a growing **Parsi** community, a small **Jewish** presence (Bene Israel and Baghdadi Jews), **British** administrators and soldiers, **Portuguese** Catholics from the pre-1661 era, and people from across the subcontinent — Bhandaris from Chaul, Vanjaras from the Ghats, Bhatias, Banias, Shenvi Brahmins, goldsmiths, ironsmiths, weavers from Gujarat.

**Frashad grew up in one of the most cosmopolitan places on earth.** Not a monoculture. A collision of languages, religions, trades, and empires.

## The Communities — How They Related

### Parsis: The British Favorites

By Frashad's birth in 1783, the Parsis had become Bombay's most strategically positioned minority. They were:

- **Shipbuilders:** The Wadia family, building warships for the EIC and Royal Navy since 1736. Over 100 warships by the 1840s. HMS Minden (1810). HMS Trincomalee (1817).
- **Merchants and traders:** Cotton, opium, and trade with China. Parsi commercial networks reached Muscat, Canton, Mozambique.
- **British allies:** The EIC actively encouraged Parsi settlement through privileges and exemptions from public duties. The Parsis' status as a minority gave them "necessary flexibility" as brokers between European traders and local populations.
- **Self-governing:** In **1787** (when Frashad is 4 years old), the British formally delegated authority over community matters to the **Bombay Parsee Panchayat**, empowering it to administer sanctions, enforce community rules, protect spousal rights, prevent bigamy, and represent the community to the government.

The Parsis were not marginalized. They were not oppressed. They were the most upwardly mobile community in Bombay — wealthy, educated, integrated into British colonial infrastructure, and fiercely protective of their religious identity. The Panchayat enforced strict internal discipline: bundubusts (community rules) were decided at public anjumans (assemblies). Violators could be expelled from community life.

**For the novel:** Frashad is born into privilege — not wealth necessarily, but community protection, social standing, and religious structure. His expulsion by the Panchayat is devastating precisely because the community he is losing is so tightly organized and so powerful. Being cast out of the Parsi community in 1801 is not like leaving a neighborhood. It is like losing citizenship in a nation.

### Jews: The Other Merchant Minority

Bombay had two distinct Jewish communities:

**The Bene Israel** — native Jews from Konkan villages south of Bombay, known as "Shanivar Teli" (Saturday oil-pressers) because they abstained from work on the Sabbath. They had lived in western India for centuries, possibly millennia. The first Bene Israel community member moved from the Konkan to Bombay in **1746**. Their first synagogue — **"The Gate of Mercy"** — was established in **1796** (when Frashad is 13).

**The Baghdadi Jews** — Sephardic Jews from Iraq and the Middle East. The first, Joseph Semah, arrived from Surat in **1730**. The most famous arrival was **David Sassoon**, who fled Baghdad in 1832 and established the banking and trading dynasty that would become one of the wealthiest families in the British Empire.

**Parsi-Jewish relations:** Both communities occupied similar positions — merchant minorities, neither Hindu nor Muslim, tolerated by the British as useful intermediaries. Academic research places them side by side as the two communities that most contributed to Bombay's industrialization. They shared no theology, but they shared a structural position: outsiders who thrived by being useful to the ruling power.

**For the novel:** Frashad would have known Jews in Bombay. A healer's son in a cosmopolitan port city would have encountered Bene Israel families. The Gate of Mercy synagogue was built when he was 13 — the same period he is learning his father's trade. Two ancient religions, both survivors, both minority communities maintaining identity against surrounding majorities. The Zoroastrian concept of cosmic exile and the Jewish concept of diaspora are eerily parallel.

### Hindus: The Majority

Hindus were the overwhelming majority in Bombay and the surrounding Maratha territories. The city's original name — Mumbai — comes from the Hindu goddess Mumbadevi. Hindu temples dotted the landscape. Hindu merchants, Brahmin administrators, and laborers from across western India formed the city's backbone.

The Parsi-Hindu relationship was generally cooperative. The founding legend of the Parsis in India — "Sugar in the Milk" — is about promising the Hindu king to dissolve into his kingdom without displacing anyone. Parsis adopted Gujarati, local dress, and dietary customs. They did not proselytize. In exchange, they were permitted to practice their religion and maintain their fire temples.

**For the novel:** Frashad grows up surrounded by Hindu neighbors. He knows Hindu festivals, Hindu dietary practices, the Hindu concept of vegetarianism. His father — a healer — may treat Hindu patients. The boundaries between communities are real but permeable in daily life.

### Muslims: The Complex Neighbor

Bombay's Muslim population was diverse — Sunni merchants, Shia traders from Persia, Arab lascars (sailors) on the docks, Siddis (Africans brought as slaves or soldiers). The adhan (call to prayer) sounded five times daily across the city. Islamic commerce was woven into the harbor's economy.

**Parsi-Muslim relations were the most fraught.** The documented communal violence in 19th-century Bombay was primarily between Parsis and Muslims — not Hindus and Muslims. The major riots occurred in **1851** and **1874**, both triggered by Parsi publications depicting the Prophet Muhammad.

But the deeper tension was theological and historical. Zoroastrians had been conquered and subjugated by Muslim armies since the 7th century. The very reason Parsis existed in India was because they fled Muslim Persia. The Arabic term *Majusi* (Zoroastrian) was deployed as a slur against Shia Muslims. Parsi Bombay was cosmopolitan enough to cooperate with Muslim neighbors, but the community's memory was long.

**For the novel:** A smart, literate Parsi boy in 1790s Bombay grew up hearing the adhan five times a day, knowing the basic kalimah, recognizing the gestures of salat. Islam was ambient, not studied. When Frashad later takes the name Fadl Allah and performs Islam to survive, he is deploying knowledge absorbed from childhood — not learned in captivity. The performance is convincing because the material was always there.

### The British: The Power

The British in Bombay were administrators, soldiers, merchants, and sailors. The EIC governor ruled from the Fort. British law applied in many cases, but community panchayats handled internal matters for each religious group. The relationship was colonial but collaborative — especially with the Parsis, who built their ships and brokered their trade.

**Governor Jonathan Duncan** (1795–1811) — the governor during Frashad's youth — served for 16 years, the longest tenure of any Bombay governor. He directed expeditions against Mysore, Egypt, and the Marathas. He oversaw the pacification of Gujarat and Kathiawar. He was present for the launch of HMS Minden in 1810.

## What Was Happening in India (1780–1820) — The Wars Around Bombay

Frashad was born into a region at war. India during this period was a battleground between the British, the Maratha Confederacy, the Kingdom of Mysore, and the remnants of Mughal power.

### The Anglo-Mysore Wars (1767–1799)

**Hyder Ali** and his son **Tipu Sultan** ("the Tiger of Mysore") fought four wars against the British. The Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780–1784) **coincided with the American Revolution** — Britain was fighting on two continents simultaneously. Tipu Sultan pioneered iron-cased rocket artillery (Mysorean rockets), used a French-trained army, and allied with France against the British.

In **1799**, the British stormed Tipu Sultan's capital at Srirangapatna. Tipu died fighting on the walls. His defeat consolidated British supremacy in southern India.

**For the novel:** When Frashad is born in 1783, Britain is losing the American Revolution *and* fighting Tipu Sultan. The British Empire is stretched across three continents. Bombay is a military staging ground as much as a trading port.

### The Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818)

The Maratha Confederacy — the most powerful Hindu political entity in India — fought three wars against the British:

| War | Dates | Key Events | Relevance |
|-----|-------|------------|-----------|
| First | 1775–1782 | British acquire Salsette island (adjacent to Bombay) via Treaty of Salbai, 1782 | Frashad is born the year after this treaty. The territory around Bombay is still being contested |
| Second | 1803–1805 | Peshwa Baji Rao II accepts British protection (Treaty of Bassein, Dec 1802). British gain first mainland territory in the Konkan | Frashad is 20. The mainland around Bombay comes under British control for the first time |
| Third | 1817–1818 | British defeat the Marathas decisively. Maratha territories annexed to Bombay Presidency. British supremacy in India established | Frashad is 34–35, already gone from Bombay, but the world he left has been utterly transformed |

### The Gujarat Famine (1790)

When Frashad is 7, a famine devastates Gujarat — the ancestral homeland of most Bombay Parsis. Waves of refugees flood into Bombay. The Parsi population **quadruples** between 1780 and 1812, largely driven by this migration. The city Frashad grows up in is rapidly expanding, crowded with new arrivals, straining its infrastructure.

### The Qawasim Pirates and the Persian Gulf (1809–1820)

Covered in detail in RESEARCH-NOVEL-DEVELOPMENT-NOTES.md. The EIC launches two campaigns from Bombay against Qawasim pirates — 1809 and 1819. These are the sea lanes that connect Bombay to Muscat, Basra, and the Persian Gulf. If Frashad is captured, this is where it happens.

## What Frashad Would Have Known

A healer's son from an Athornan family, growing up in Bombay between 1783 and 1801, would have known:

- **Multiple languages:** Gujarati (home), Marathi (streets), possibly some Hindi, some English (docks), some Arabic (harbor). Islam was ambient — the adhan five times a day, the gestures of salat, the basic kalimah
- **Multiple religions practiced side by side:** Hindu temples, Muslim mosques, Parsi fire temples, the new Jewish synagogue (1796), Portuguese Catholic churches
- **The docks:** Ships being built by his community. British warships. Arab dhows. The smell of teak, tar, rope, salt water. British surgeons arriving on Parsi-built ships
- **The wars:** Tipu Sultan's defeat (1799), the Maratha conflicts, Napoleonic Wars creating ripple effects in Bombay's harbor. Soldiers and sailors from everywhere
- **The famine refugees:** Parsis arriving from Gujarat when he was 7, crowding into the community, stretching resources, expanding the world he knew
- **The Panchayat:** The institution that governed his community, enforced the rules, settled disputes, represented Parsis to the British. The institution with the power to cast him out
- **His father's patients:** Hindus, Muslims, Parsis, possibly Jews — a healer in a cosmopolitan city treats everyone. Frashad would have seen suffering across every religious boundary before he was 10 years old

*He left this world at 18. He carried it inside him for the rest of his life. Every language he learned afterward, every disguise he wore, every prayer he performed — it was layered on top of the Bombay he could never go back to.*

*The fire temple where his father prayed. The harbor where the ships were built. The boy at the dock. The Panchayat that destroyed him.*

*All of it still burning inside him when he stood in the bow of a whaleboat 10,000 miles away, killing the guardians of the Tree of Life.*
