# Chapter References & Scene Analysis

*Compiled for FRASHAD: A Novel*

*Key scenes from Moby-Dick analyzed from Fedallah/Frashad's perspective.*

---

## Chapter 36: "The Quarter-Deck" — The Oath

### What Happens

Ahab assembles the entire crew on the quarter-deck after days of restless pacing. He reveals his true purpose: not a commercial whaling voyage but a personal hunt for Moby Dick, the white whale that took his leg. He nails a gold Spanish doubloon to the mast — reward for whoever first spots the whale.

Then the ritual:

1. Ahab orders the three harpooneers — Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo — to detach the iron heads from their harpoons
2. The mates (Starbuck, Stubb, Flask) use the hollow harpoon sockets as cups
3. Ahab fills them with grog
4. The harpooneers drink from these makeshift cups
5. They chant: **"Death to Moby Dick!"**
6. Ahab has the mates cross their lances before him and grasps them at the crossing point
7. A mug is passed around the full crew

Starbuck alone objects: "I came here to hunt whales, not my commander's vengeance." Ahab overrules him. Starbuck gives in.

### Where Is Fedallah?

**Below decks. Hidden.** Fedallah and his four men are still concealed in the hold. They don't emerge until Chapters 47–48, during the first whale chase. During the Quarter-Deck scene, Melville notes **"a low laugh from the hold"** — Fedallah is down there, listening, but unseen.

He is not part of this ritual. He does not drink. He does not chant "Death to Moby Dick." He does not take the public oath.

### Why This Matters

The three harpooneers — Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo — are recruited into Ahab's obsession through spectacle: grog, ceremony, group pressure, the gold doubloon, the charisma of a madman giving orders. It works. They shout the oath. They're in.

Fedallah doesn't need any of this. His commitment to Ahab predates the voyage. Whatever passed between them — in Nantucket, on the docks, in the dark before sailing — it was settled in private, in silence. No grog. No crossed lances. No witnesses.

The other three were recruited. Fedallah chose.

And he's listening from below. He hears Ahab bend the entire crew to his will. He hears Starbuck resist and fail. He hears three men from three continents pledge themselves to a hunt they don't understand. He already knows how it ends.

The low laugh from the hold — is it amusement? Recognition? The sound of a man who has heard oaths before, in fire temples and on dhow decks and in ports where oaths are cheap, and who knows what oaths are worth?

## The Grog Question: Would Fedallah Drink?

### The Zoroastrian Position

**Alcohol is NOT forbidden in Zoroastrianism.** This is one of the sharp differences from Islam.

- Wine is *sacred* in Zoroastrian tradition. The Avestan word is **madho**.
- Pure grape wine plays a central role in religious rituals
- When the sacred **haoma** plant (a ritual drink used in the culminating Ab-Zohr ceremony) wasn't available, grape wine was substituted
- Wine was prescribed as a tonic, permitted among the priesthood, used in ceremonies
- Wine is said to "unveil the true nature of individuals"
- What IS forbidden: **drunkenness** and the mixing of various drinks together
- Moderate drinking of pure grape wine = permitted, even honored

If Frashad were still a practicing Parsee, drinking wine would be perfectly normal. Even expected at Gahanbar festivals and religious occasions.

### The Islamic Position

**Alcohol is absolutely haram (forbidden) in Islam.** The Quran is explicit:

> "O you who have believed, indeed, intoxicants, gambling, [sacrificing on] stone altars [to other than Allah], and divining arrows are but defilement from the work of Satan, so avoid it that you may be successful."

Khamr (wine) is forbidden. This is among the clearest prohibitions in the religion. A Muslim man seen drinking is noticed, judged, and potentially endangered in a strict community.

### The Tension for Frashad

When Frashad takes the name Fedallah and moves through the Muslim trading world — Zanzibar, Muscat, the East African coast, the dhow routes — he's presenting as Muslim. His Arabic name opens doors. His fluent Arabic passes scrutiny.

Does he drink?

- **If he drinks:** it risks blowing his cover. A "Muslim" man drinking in a port town is noticed. The wrong eyes see it and the disguise frays.
- **If he doesn't drink:** he's observing an Islamic prohibition that has nothing to do with his actual religion. He's performing someone else's piety. A Zoroastrian abstaining from wine to maintain a Muslim disguise — layers of irony.
- **On the Pequod:** the grog ration is standard on American whaling ships. Sailors drink daily. A man who refuses would be marked as strange — one more piece of Fedallah's alienness for the crew to whisper about.

### The Behāfarīd Echo

Behāfarīd — the Zoroastrian heretic prophet whose followers Frashad might echo — prohibited wine and intoxicants. This was one of his Islamic-influenced reforms. If Frashad's exile connects to the Behāfarīd tradition (unauthorized prophecy, heretical reform), his relationship to alcohol might carry that mark: a Zoroastrian who doesn't drink, not because of Islam, but because of a heretical strain within his own tradition.

### Three Possibilities for the Novel

**1. He drinks and doesn't care.**
He left Zoroastrianism's framework when he was expelled. He never adopted Islam's rules sincerely. He drinks what's put in front of him because he stopped performing anyone's piety decades ago. The man who let his hair grow into a crown of nasu — who wears his own ritual impurity as a headdress — is not worried about wine. He is past all rules. He is past caring.

**2. He doesn't drink, and it's one more thing that makes him alien.**
He never explains why. The crew reads it as more evidence of his devil-nature — even the grog won't pass his "steel-like lips." In reality, it might be:
- The last Zoroastrian discipline he still observes (the prohibition against drunkenness, taken to its extreme)
- Old muscle memory from years performing Islam in the ports — a habit that outlasted the disguise
- A private discipline: the man who sees visions doesn't cloud them. He needs clarity. He needs to see what's coming.

**3. He drinks, but only wine, and only moderately — the Zoroastrian way.**
Pure grape wine. Never to drunkenness. Never mixed. The religion says it "unveils the true nature of individuals." Maybe that's exactly what he wants — to see truly. The wine doesn't cloud his visions. It feeds them. No one on the Pequod notices the distinction. They see a man drinking or not drinking. They don't see the theology behind the glass.

### The Deeper Question

Whatever Frashad does with alcohol, the real question is: **how much of his original self survives?**

He left Bombay at eighteen. He's been on the ocean for thirty-four years by the time the Pequod sails. He has shed his name, his community, his dress, his hair discipline, his fire, his Kusti cord (or has he?), his daily prayers (or do they still come involuntarily at dawn?).

What's left?

Every detail like this — does he drink, does he pray, does he still tie the Kusti in secret, does he still flinch when someone breathes on a flame — is a marker of how far from Bombay he's traveled, and how much of the boy who tended sacred fire is still alive inside the man the crew calls the devil.

The novel is built from these details. Each one is a measure of erosion.

## Chapter 47–48: "The Mat-Maker" / "The First Lowering" — Fedallah Emerges

### What Happens

The first whale is spotted. Boats are lowered. And from the hold emerge **five men** no one knew were aboard — "five dusky phantoms" led by Fedallah. This is Ahab's secret boat crew, smuggled on before sailing.

The crew is shocked. "Who are these men?" Fedallah takes his place in the bow of Ahab's boat as harpooner.

### From Frashad's Perspective

He has been below decks for weeks — since before the ship sailed from Nantucket. Hidden. Waiting. Listening to the ship's sounds: the crew above, the water below, Ahab's ivory leg tapping the quarterdeck.

Then the call: a whale sighted. And he climbs into daylight for the first time since Nantucket, stands in the bow of a whaleboat, picks up a harpoon, and becomes visible.

This is the moment the crew first sees him. Everything they will think about him — devil, familiar spirit, evil angel — begins here. Their first impression becomes permanent.

**What does HE see?** Three other harpooneers in three other boats — Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo. Brown men, like him. Foreign men, like him. Men who kill for white captains, like him. Does he look at them? Does he recognize anything?

And behind him in the boat: Ahab. The one-legged man. The man he followed across the world. The man he will die before.

## Chapter 117: "The Whale Watch" — The Prophecy

### What Happens

Night. Ahab and Fedallah sit together in Ahab's whale boat, watching over a killed whale lashed to the ship. Everyone else is asleep or on the ship. It's just the two of them, in a small boat, on the dark water.

Fedallah delivers the three prophecies:

1. **Two hearses:** "Ere thou couldst die on this voyage, two hearses must verily be seen by thee on the sea; the first not made by mortal hands; and the visible wood of the last one must be grown in America."
2. **Fedallah dies first:** "Though it come to the last, I shall still go before thee thy pilot."
3. **Hemp:** "Hemp only can kill thee."

Ahab takes these to mean he's essentially immortal — no hearses at sea, no American hearse, and hemp means the gallows, which he'll never face. He's wrong about all of it.

### From Frashad's Perspective

This is the most intimate scene between them in the entire novel. Two men in a boat at night. One speaks. One listens.

**Does Frashad know what the prophecies mean?** When he says "I shall still go before thee thy pilot," does he know he's describing his own death — his body lashed to the whale, riding it as a figurehead, piloting Ahab toward the end? Or do the prophecies come through him the way they always have — involuntary, like the dawn prayers, like the visions that got him expelled from Bombay?

**This is the first time in the novel that Fedallah speaks at length.** Everything else is silence. Here, finally, he opens his mouth and tells the truth. And Ahab doesn't understand it.

This is the pattern of his life. He speaks truth. No one hears it correctly. In Bombay, they heard sorcery. On the Pequod, Ahab hears immortality. The content is always the same. The reception is always wrong.

## Chapter 119: "The Candles" — Fire

### What Happens

A typhoon. St. Elmo's fire (electrical discharge) blazes on the ship's three masts — pale, ghostly flames dancing on the tips. The crew is terrified.

Ahab addresses the fire directly:

> "Oh! thou clear spirit of clear fire, whom on these seas I as Persian once did worship, till in the sacramental act so burned by thee, that to this hour I bear the scar."

He puts his foot on Fedallah — the text says he "placed his foot upon the Parsee" — and addresses the fire as a former Zoroastrian might address sacred flame.

### From Frashad's Perspective

Ahab says "as Persian once did worship." This implies Ahab has some past connection to Zoroastrian fire worship — almost certainly through Fedallah. At some point, Fedallah taught Ahab something about fire. About Asha. About the sacred flame.

And now Ahab is standing on him — literally — while addressing that fire. The captain uses the Parsee's body as a platform and the Parsee's religion as a speech.

**What does Frashad feel?** A man who tended the Atash Bahram as a child, who fed sacred fire with sandalwood, who was taught that fire is the visible manifestation of truth — watching St. Elmo's fire dance on the masts of a doomed ship while a madman stands on his back and shouts at the sky.

Is this Asha? Is this Druj? Is there a difference anymore?

The fire on the masts is not sacred. It's electrical. It's physics. But it looks like the fire he knew. It looks like the fire temple at dawn when his father had not arrived yet and he tended the flame alone and everything in the universe seemed briefly to be in its right place.

It looks like home. And it is the furthest thing from home he has ever seen.

## Chapter 125: "The Log and Line" — Ahab Meets Pip

### What Happens

Pip wanders on deck, still mad from his abandonment in the ocean. Ahab encounters him and is cracked open:

> "Oh, ye frozen heavens! look down here. Ye did beget this luckless child, and have abandoned him, ye creative libertines. Here, boy; Ahab's cabin shall be Pip's home henceforth, while Ahab lives."

> "Thou touchest my inmost centre, boy; thou art tied to me by cords woven of my heart-strings."

### From Frashad's Perspective

Fedallah has been Ahab's shadow for the entire voyage. He has stood behind Ahab on the quarterdeck at night. He has delivered prophecy. He has been the closest person to the captain.

And now a mad boy walks on deck and reaches something in Ahab that Fedallah never could.

**Does Fedallah see this as a threat?** As a relief? As confirmation of something he already knew — that Ahab's humanity was still in there, buried under the monomania, and that the one thing that could pull it out was not prophecy or fire worship or the Zoroastrian cosmic framework, but simple human tenderness from a broken child?

**The parallel:** Frashad was expelled from his community. Pip was abandoned in the ocean. Both were cast out — declared worthless by the systems they lived in. Frashad was called a yatu. Pip was valued at one-thirtieth of a whale. Both survived. Both arrived at Ahab broken.

But Ahab takes Pip in. Ahab never takes Fedallah in — not like that. Fedallah is useful. Fedallah is his shadow, his prophet, his harpooner. But "thou touchest my inmost centre" — that, Ahab says to Pip.

Does Fedallah hear it? Does he care?

Or has he moved past needing to be loved? Is that what thirty-four years of exile does — it burns away the need to be touched at the centre, until what's left is a man who can stand in the bow of a boat and throw a harpoon and see the future and feel nothing at all about any of it?

## Chapter 129: "The Cabin" — Ahab Locks Pip Away

### What Happens

Ahab tries to leave Pip behind before the final chase. Pip begs to come:

> "Sir, do ye but use poor me for your one lost leg."

Ahab refuses — not from cruelty but because Pip threatens his monomania:

> "There is that in thee, poor lad, which I feel too curing to my malady. Like cures like; and for this hunt, my malady becomes my most desired health."

He locks Pip in the cabin. Tells him he can play captain. Says goodbye:

> "God for ever bless thee; and if it come to that — God for ever save thee, let what will befall."

### From Frashad's Perspective

Ahab chooses the whale over the boy. He chooses death over healing. He chooses Fedallah's prophecy over Pip's love.

This is the moment the ship's fate is sealed. Not when Ahab nailed the doubloon. Not when the harpooneers drank from their lances. Here — when the one human being who could have cured him is locked in a cabin and left behind.

Fedallah is on deck. He knows. The final chase is coming. Pip is locked below. Ahab is walking toward him.

There is nothing left to do but go.

## Chapters 133–135: "The Chase" — Three Days

### The Structure

**Day 1:** Moby Dick is sighted. Three boats lower. Moby Dick destroys Ahab's boat. Ahab is rescued. Fedallah survives.

**Day 2:** The chase continues. Moby Dick destroys Ahab's boat again. Fedallah is caught in the whale lines. **He does not resurface.** When the crew is recovered, Fedallah is missing.

**Day 3:** Moby Dick surfaces with **Fedallah's corpse lashed to its body** — tangled in harpoon lines, pinioned to the whale. The first hearse: "not made by mortal hands." Ahab sees him:

> "Aye, Parsee! I see thee again. — Aye, and thou goest before; and this, THIS then is the hearse that thou didst promise."

Ahab throws his last harpoon. The line catches around his neck. He is shot out of the boat. Moby Dick rams the Pequod. The ship sinks. Tashtego nails the flag to the mast, catching a sky-hawk. Everything goes down.

Ishmael floats. Queequeg's coffin surfaces. The Rachel rescues him.

### From Frashad's Perspective — Day 2: The Death

This is the scene the novel FRASHAD must write.

Fedallah goes into the water. He is tangled in lines. The whale is pulling him down.

What does he experience?

- Does he struggle?
- Does he let go?
- Does the body fight while the mind accepts?
- Do the Avestan prayers come — Yatha Ahu Vairyo, Ashem Vohu — one last time, involuntary, the way they always have at dawn?
- Does he see the white shape that he's been seeing since Lahaina — the shape in dark water — and recognize it finally as this moment, the moment the visions were always pointing toward?
- Does he think of his father?
- Does he think of Bombay?
- Does he think of the fire?

His last word, in the novel outline: **Asha.**

*The order is restored. I return to truth. I am going home.*

No one on the Pequod understands it.
